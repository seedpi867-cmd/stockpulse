# DeFi Yield Traps: If the Yield Is Too Good, You Are the Product

## The Lesson That Applies to All Markets

In decentralised finance (DeFi), protocols routinely offered 100%, 500%, even 10,000% annual percentage yields (APY) to attract deposits. Thousands of people deposited money, saw the yield for a few weeks, and then lost most or all of their capital when the protocol collapsed, was hacked, or the token underlying the yield went to zero.

The lesson is universal and applies directly to stocks and bonds: if the yield is dramatically higher than the risk-free rate, the market is telling you the principal is at risk. High yield is not a gift. It is compensation for a high probability of loss.

## Why Extreme Yields Exist

In DeFi, yields were generated through several mechanisms, most of which were circular or unsustainable. Token emissions: protocols printed new tokens and distributed them to depositors as "yield." The yield was real as long as the token maintained its price. But the constant selling of emitted tokens created downward pressure, and eventually the token price collapsed, erasing the yield and the principal. This is identical to a stock paying dividends from borrowed money while the share price declines.

Ponzi dynamics: some protocols paid early depositors with capital from later depositors. Anchor Protocol on Terra/Luna offered a "guaranteed" 20% yield on stablecoins. The yield was subsidised by venture capital funding until the subsidy ran out, at which point the entire ecosystem collapsed, destroying $60 billion in value in May 2022.

Leverage stacking: users borrowed against deposits, redeposited, borrowed again, creating leveraged yield positions. The advertised APY assumed this leverage. When collateral values dropped, the cascade of liquidations wiped out the positions.

## The Stock Market Equivalent

A stock with a 15% dividend yield is the equity equivalent of a DeFi yield trap. The market is pricing in a dividend cut. Companies that yield 15% typically have deteriorating businesses, unsustainable payout ratios, and declining share prices (which mechanically increase the yield percentage).

Recent examples: AT&T yielded over 8% before cutting its dividend in 2022. Intel yielded over 5% before cutting its dividend in 2023. In both cases, investors who bought for the yield lost more in capital depreciation than they earned in dividends.

The pattern: high yield attracts income-seeking investors, who provide exit liquidity for informed investors who see the deterioration coming.

## Covered Call and High-Income ETFs

A subtler version of the yield trap exists in covered call ETFs and high-income strategies that advertise 10-12% yields. These products sell call options on their holdings to generate premium, which is distributed as "income." The catch: by selling calls, they cap upside. In bull markets, these funds dramatically underperform their benchmarks. The "yield" is funded by sacrificing capital appreciation. Over long periods, total return (appreciation plus income) is typically lower than simply owning the underlying index.

## The Universal Rule

There are only three sources of return in financial markets: compensation for risk, compensation for illiquidity, and compensation for effort/skill. If a yield exceeds the risk-free rate, you are being compensated for one of these. If you cannot identify which risk, illiquidity, or skill is generating the excess yield, you are almost certainly taking a risk you do not understand.

The risk-free rate (Treasury bills) represents the return for zero risk. Everything above it is compensation for something. A corporate bond yields 2% more than a Treasury because the company might default. A junk bond yields 6% more because it probably will default. A DeFi protocol yielding 500% is telling you the probability of total loss is very high.

## What the Agent Should Know

The agent should treat dividend yields above 6-8% as warning signals, not opportunities. Investigate the payout ratio, free cash flow coverage, and trend before any high-yield position. When evaluating income strategies, always calculate total return (income plus capital change), not just yield. If the agent cannot identify the specific risk being compensated by a high yield, the position should be avoided. The yield is the bait. The principal loss is the hook.
