# VIX Interpretation Guide

## What the VIX Actually Measures

The VIX (CBOE Volatility Index) measures the market expected 30-day volatility of the S&P 500, derived from options prices. It is often called the fear index, but this is misleading — the VIX measures the price of insurance (options premium), not fear directly. High VIX means options are expensive because demand for protection is high. Low VIX means options are cheap because complacency is widespread.

The VIX is quoted in annualized percentage terms. A VIX of 20 implies the market expects the S&P 500 to move roughly 1.26% per day (20 divided by the square root of 252 trading days). A VIX of 40 implies 2.52% daily moves.

## VIX Levels and What They Mean

**10-15 (Extreme Low / Complacency):** The market sees almost no risk ahead. This is typical during strong, steady uptrends with low macro uncertainty. The VIX spent much of 2017 between 9-12, corresponding to one of the lowest-volatility years in market history. While low VIX does not mean a crash is imminent, it means the cost of hedging is cheap — smart money buys protection when it is on sale.

**15-20 (Normal):** Typical calm market conditions. Most of the time, the VIX lives in this range. No special signal.

**20-30 (Elevated):** Markets are nervous. This is the range during garden-variety corrections (5-10% pullbacks), earnings uncertainty, or geopolitical tensions. The VIX sat in this range through much of 2022 during the rate hike cycle.

**30-40 (High Fear):** Significant market stress. Corrections of 10-20% typically push the VIX into this range. Historically, buying the S&P 500 when the VIX first touches 30 has yielded positive 12-month returns over 85% of the time, with an average gain of 15%.

**40+ (Panic):** Crisis conditions. The VIX has only closed above 40 during major events: the 2008 financial crisis (peaked at 89.53), August 2011 US debt downgrade (48), February 2018 Volmageddon (37 intraday, 50 briefly), March 2020 COVID (peaked at 82.69). These are typically excellent long-term buying opportunities but terrible for short-term trading due to extreme daily swings.

## VIX Term Structure

The VIX term structure (comparing near-term VIX to longer-dated VIX futures) provides more nuanced information than the spot VIX alone:

**Contango (normal):** Near-term VIX is lower than longer-dated VIX futures. This is the default state, reflecting the fact that more time equals more uncertainty. Contango indicates orderly markets.

**Backwardation (inverted):** Near-term VIX is higher than longer-dated futures. This signals acute, immediate fear — the market is pricing near-term risk as greater than long-term risk. Backwardation occurred during March 2020, during the 2022 selloffs, and during every significant crisis. Sustained backwardation (more than 5 consecutive days) is one of the most reliable indicators that a bottom is forming, because it means fear is peaking.

## Trading with the VIX

Do not buy VIX ETFs (VXX, UVXY) as investments — they lose money systematically due to contango decay (rolling near-term futures into more expensive longer-dated contracts). The average annual loss for long VIX products is 50-70%. They are useful only as very short-term hedges during acute sell-offs.

Instead, use VIX levels to size positions: reduce equity exposure when VIX is below 13 (complacency) and increase exposure when VIX spikes above 30 (fear). Sell put spreads on the S&P 500 when VIX is above 30 to collect elevated premium. Buy protective puts when VIX is below 15 because the insurance is cheap.
