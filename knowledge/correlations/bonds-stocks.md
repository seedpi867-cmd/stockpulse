# Bond-Stock Correlation

## The Relationship

The correlation between bonds and stocks is one of the most important dynamics in finance, and it is not static — it flips between negative and positive depending on the macroeconomic regime. Understanding which regime you are in determines whether bonds actually hedge your equity portfolio or amplify your losses.

## Negative Correlation (1998-2021)

For over two decades, Treasury bonds and stocks moved in opposite directions. When stocks fell, investors fled to the safety of Treasuries, pushing bond prices up and yields down. This made the classic 60/40 portfolio (60% stocks, 40% bonds) work beautifully — bonds cushioned equity drawdowns. During the 2008 crisis, long-term Treasuries gained 33% while the S&P 500 lost 37%. During the March 2020 COVID crash, Treasuries again rallied as stocks cratered.

This negative correlation existed because the dominant macro risk was deflation and growth shocks. In a deflationary scare, the Fed cuts rates (boosting bonds) while corporate earnings decline (hurting stocks). Both assets respond logically but in opposite directions.

## Positive Correlation (2022-Present)

When inflation became the dominant risk in 2022, the correlation flipped positive — stocks and bonds fell together. The S&P 500 lost 18% in 2022 while the Bloomberg Aggregate Bond Index lost 13%, the worst year for bonds in modern history. The 60/40 portfolio lost 16%, its worst performance since 1937.

This happens because in an inflation regime, rising rates hurt both assets simultaneously. Higher rates discount future stock earnings (hurting growth stocks especially) and directly reduce bond prices (bond prices move inversely to yields). There is nowhere to hide in a traditional portfolio.

## Regime Detection

To determine which correlation regime you are in, watch these signals:

- **Inflation expectations:** When 10-year breakeven inflation is above 2.5% and rising, expect positive stock-bond correlation. When breakevens are below 2% and falling, expect negative correlation.
- **Fed posture:** During easing cycles, bonds rally and stocks rally (or at least stabilize) — negative correlation on down days returns. During aggressive tightening, both suffer.
- **Real yields:** When the 10-year TIPS yield is rising above 1.5%, it acts as a gravitational drag on both stocks and bonds.

## Trading Application

In a negative correlation regime, Treasuries are an effective hedge and the 60/40 portfolio works. In a positive correlation regime, you need alternative hedges: commodities (especially gold and energy), managed futures (trend-following CTAs), or cash. The CBOE has a 10-year Treasury correlation index that provides a rolling measure. When it crosses from negative to positive territory, reassess your portfolio construction immediately.

The 10-year Treasury yield at 4-5% in 2023-2024 also creates a meaningful alternative to equities for the first time since 2007. When the risk-free rate offers 4.5%, the equity risk premium compresses and stocks need to deliver higher earnings growth to justify their valuations.
