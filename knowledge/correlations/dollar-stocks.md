# US Dollar and Stock Market Correlation

## The Inverse Relationship

The US Dollar Index (DXY) and the S&P 500 generally exhibit a negative correlation — when the dollar strengthens, US stocks tend to weaken, and vice versa. This relationship is not absolute but has held roughly 60-70% of the time over the past two decades.

## Why the Dollar Matters for Stocks

**Earnings translation:** Roughly 40% of S&P 500 revenue comes from overseas. When the dollar strengthens, foreign revenue translates back into fewer dollars, directly reducing reported earnings. A 10% rise in the DXY typically reduces S&P 500 earnings by 3-5%. In 2022, the DXY surged from 96 to 114 (18.75%), and companies like Microsoft, Procter and Gamble, and IBM all cited currency headwinds of 4-6% on earnings calls.

**Commodity prices:** Most global commodities are priced in US dollars. A stronger dollar makes commodities more expensive for foreign buyers, reducing demand and pressuring prices. This directly impacts energy, materials, and mining stocks. The inverse: a weakening dollar is bullish for commodity producers and emerging markets.

**Capital flows:** A strengthening dollar attracts foreign capital into US assets (especially Treasuries), but also increases the real debt burden for emerging market countries that borrowed in dollars. This can trigger EM currency crises (Turkey 2018, Argentina 2018) that create contagion risk.

## When the Correlation Breaks

The dollar-stock negative correlation breaks during risk-off panics when both the dollar and US stocks move in unexpected ways. During acute global crises, the dollar can rally (as the global reserve currency and safe haven) even as US stocks fall — this was the pattern in March 2020. In this scenario, the dollar is not rising because of US strength but because of global fear.

Conversely, during US-specific crises (debt ceiling standoffs, domestic political turmoil), both the dollar and US stocks can fall together as global investors reduce all US exposure.

## Sector Sensitivity

Not all stocks respond equally to dollar moves:
- **Most sensitive (negative):** Multinational tech (Apple, Microsoft, Google), consumer staples with global exposure (Coca-Cola, P&G), industrials with export revenue (Caterpillar, Boeing)
- **Least sensitive or positive:** Domestic-focused companies (utilities, regional banks, healthcare providers, homebuilders) actually benefit from a strong dollar through lower import costs
- **Commodity producers:** Energy and mining stocks benefit from dollar weakness through higher commodity prices

## Trading the DXY

The DXY is a trade-weighted index dominated by the Euro (57.6% weight), followed by the Japanese Yen (13.6%), British Pound (11.9%), Canadian Dollar (9.1%), Swedish Krona (4.2%), and Swiss Franc (3.6%). Because the Euro dominates, DXY movements are largely a mirror of EUR/USD. A European recession that weakens the Euro will mechanically strengthen the DXY even if US conditions are unchanged.

Watch DXY at key technical levels (100, 105, 110) and the rate differential between US and European/Japanese central banks. When the Fed is hiking while the ECB and BOJ hold steady, the dollar strengthens and creates a headwind for multinational earnings. The reverse — Fed cuts while others hold — weakens the dollar and provides a tailwind.
