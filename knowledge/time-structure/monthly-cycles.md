# Monthly Cycles — The Calendar Flows Nobody Tells Retail About

## The Turn of the Month Effect

The last trading day of each month and the first three trading days of the next month consistently exhibit stronger-than-average returns. This "turn of the month" (TOM) effect is one of the most robust calendar anomalies, documented across dozens of international markets and persisting for decades.

The mechanism is straightforward: systematic money flows.

- **401(k) and pension contributions.** Payroll deductions for retirement accounts are typically processed at month-end or the first business day of the new month. This creates automatic buying pressure in equity index funds and target-date funds. The amounts are enormous — total US retirement assets exceed $35 trillion, and a significant portion receives monthly contributions.
- **Automatic investment plans.** Dollar-cost averaging programs, robo-advisors, and systematic investment plans typically execute on the first of the month or the first business day.
- **Mutual fund subscriptions.** Monthly subscription flows into mutual funds are typically processed at the first NAV calculation of the new month.

The TOM effect is strongest in large-cap index components (S&P 500 stocks) because most passive inflows go into index funds. It is weaker in small-caps and non-index stocks.

## Options Expiration Week

Standard monthly options expire on the third Friday of each month. The week leading up to and including options expiration (OpEx) has distinct characteristics:

**Pinning.** As expiration approaches, stocks with heavy open interest at specific strike prices tend to be "pinned" to those strikes. This occurs because options dealers (market makers) who sold options must continuously hedge by buying stock when it dips below the strike and selling when it rises above. This hedging activity pushes the stock back toward the strike — pinning it.

**Gamma effects.** In the final days before expiration, options have maximum gamma (sensitivity of delta to price changes). This means dealer hedging becomes more aggressive, amplifying any price movement. The result: increased volatility in the days immediately preceding expiration, followed by a volatility drop after expiration.

**Max pain.** The price at which the maximum number of options (puts and calls combined) expire worthless. Options dealers benefit when options expire worthless. There is debate about whether max pain exerts genuine gravitational pull on prices, but the concept is worth monitoring — stocks do tend to settle near max pain levels as expiration approaches.

**Quarterly "quad witching."** Four times per year (third Fridays of March, June, September, December), stock options, stock index options, stock index futures, and single stock futures all expire simultaneously. These days see massive volume (often 50-100% above average) and unusual price action driven by rolling and hedging flows. The moves are often noise, not signal.

## End of Month Window Dressing

Fund managers whose performance is evaluated monthly face incentives to "window dress" their portfolios at month-end:

- **Buy recent winners.** Adding stocks that have performed well makes the portfolio look like it participated in the month's trends. These buys create buying pressure in momentum stocks at month-end.
- **Sell recent losers.** Removing underperformers before month-end reporting makes the portfolio look cleaner. This creates selling pressure in lagging stocks.
- **Both effects reverse.** The window-dressing buying and selling tends to partially reverse in the first days of the new month, contributing to the turn-of-month pattern.

## End of Quarter Intensification

Quarter-end (March 31, June 30, September 30, December 31) amplifies all monthly effects because:

- Mutual fund reports are published quarterly, intensifying window-dressing incentives
- Index reconstitution trades occur (S&P 500 rebalances quarterly)
- 13F filing positions are established (see 13f-filings.md) — funds that want to show certain holdings as of quarter-end must own them by the last day
- Tax-loss harvesting concentrates in Q4 (especially December) as investors sell losing positions for tax benefits
- January effect: small-cap stocks historically outperform in January as tax-loss selling pressure from December reverses

## For the Agent

- **Days -1 through +3 of each month:** expect a mild bullish tailwind from systematic flows. This is not a trade signal but a contextual bias that can tip borderline decisions toward long.
- **OpEx week (week of third Friday):** expect increased volatility and potential pinning around high-open-interest strikes for individual positions. Avoid initiating options positions that expire in the current week unless specifically trading the expiration dynamics.
- **Quarter-end:** heightened volatility, rebalancing flows, and window dressing. Expect momentum stocks to be stronger than normal in the last 3 days of the quarter and weaker in the first 3 days of the next quarter.
- **December:** tax-loss selling creates temporary weakness in stocks that are already down for the year. These forced sellers create opportunities in fundamentally sound stocks that are down for non-fundamental reasons.
