# Survivorship Bias: The Graveyard You Never Visit

## The Trap

You study Warren Buffett, Peter Lynch, and George Soros. You read their books, analyse their strategies, and attempt to replicate their methods. What you do not see are the thousands of fund managers who used similar approaches and went bankrupt. You are studying the survivors and mistaking survival for a repeatable strategy.

Survivorship bias is the logical error of concentrating on people or things that passed some selection process while overlooking those that did not, typically because the failures are invisible. In financial markets, survivorship bias is everywhere and it systematically distorts every piece of research, every backtest, and every historical comparison.

## In Stock Selection

You look at Amazon: $5 in 1997, over $3,000 at its peak. You think "if only I had bought Amazon." But Amazon is the survivor. During the same period, hundreds of internet companies launched with similar ambitions: Pets.com, Webvan, eToys, Kozmo.com, Flooz. They all went to zero. Amazon's success was not inevitable — it survived a near-death experience in 2001 when the stock dropped 93%. For every Amazon, there are a hundred dead companies you have never heard of because they do not appear in any success narrative.

The same applies to sectors. "Tech stocks are the best long-term investment" is survivorship bias. The tech stocks that exist today are the survivors. The thousands of tech companies that failed, delisted, or were acquired at losses are not in the index.

## In Index Construction

The S&P 500's long-term track record is inflated by survivorship bias. The index is not static — the committee removes failing companies and adds successful ones. When General Electric was removed in 2018 after a decade of decline, its poor performance was replaced by a growing company. The index perpetually refreshes itself with winners and discards losers, making its historical returns look better than what a buy-and-hold investor in the original 500 companies would have experienced.

Research by Dimensional Fund Advisors found that of the original companies in the S&P 500 when it was created in 1957, fewer than 100 remained in the index by 2013. The index's returns reflect constant curation, not passive holding.

## In Hedge Fund Performance

Hedge fund industry returns are severely distorted by survivorship bias. When a fund performs poorly, it closes and stops reporting returns. Databases of hedge fund performance only contain funds that are still alive. Studies estimate that survivorship bias inflates reported hedge fund industry returns by 2-4% per year. The actual average hedge fund underperforms a simple index fund, but survivorship bias in the data hides this.

## In Trading Strategy Backtests

Every backtest you see online is contaminated by survivorship bias unless specifically addressed. If you backtest a strategy on "all current S&P 500 stocks" going back 20 years, you are only including companies that survived long enough to still be in the index today. You are excluding every bankruptcy, delisting, and acquisition that would have damaged the strategy's returns. A proper backtest uses a survivorship-bias-free dataset that includes delisted securities — these are harder to obtain and more expensive.

## In Trading Education

"This trader made $10 million" attracts a YouTube audience. "This trader lost his house" does not get shared. Trading education is dominated by survivors sharing what worked for them. The base rate for retail trading profitability is grim: academic studies consistently find that 70-90% of retail day traders lose money. The success stories you see are the statistical minority, amplified by media incentives.

## What the Agent Should Know

The agent must apply survivorship bias correction to all historical analysis. When evaluating a strategy, ask what happened to the cases that failed. When evaluating a stock based on historical comparables, include the companies that did not survive. When reading industry performance statistics, check whether the data source includes dead funds and delisted securities. The winners are visible. The losers are invisible. The invisible losers usually outnumber the visible winners by a large margin.
