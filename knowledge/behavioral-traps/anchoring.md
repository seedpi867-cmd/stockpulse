# Anchoring: The Price Tag in Your Head That Kills You

## The Trap

You bought NVDA at $800. It drops to $500. You hold because $800 is your reference point — your anchor. You think "it was just at $800, it will come back." But the market does not know your entry price. It does not care. $800 is a number that exists only in your head, and it is distorting every decision you make about the position.

Anchoring is a cognitive bias identified by Tversky and Kahneman (1974) in which people rely too heavily on the first piece of information they encounter (the "anchor") when making subsequent judgements. In trading, the anchor is almost always your purchase price, a recent high, or a round number.

## How Anchoring Destroys Traders

The damage manifests in several predictable ways.

Holding losers too long: you anchor to your entry price and refuse to sell below it, waiting to "get back to even." The stock drops from $800 to $500 to $300. At each step you think "I have already lost so much, I might as well wait for the recovery." The recovery may never come. Stocks can go to zero. Your anchor turns a manageable loss into a catastrophic one.

Missing the exit on winners: you bought at $100, set a mental target of $200 because the stock was at $200 a year ago. It gets to $180 and you hold for the last $20. It reverses to $120. Your anchor ($200) was an arbitrary historical price that had no bearing on the stock's current fair value.

Averaging down irrationally: "It was $80 last month, so $50 is cheap." No. $50 might be expensive if the business is deteriorating. Cheapness is relative to intrinsic value, not to previous prices.

## Round Number Anchoring

Markets themselves exhibit anchoring around round numbers. Stocks hover near $100, $50, $200. Indices stall at 4,000, 5,000, 40,000. These levels have no fundamental significance — $99.95 and $100.05 represent the same company — but traders cluster orders around them, creating real support and resistance. This is collective anchoring on a mass scale.

The S&P 500 at 4,000 was a meaningless number fundamentally but it acted as a magnet and a barrier for months because millions of market participants used it as a reference point.

## The Professional Antidote

Professional traders deal with anchoring by asking one question: "If I had no position, would I enter this trade today at this price?" If the answer is no, the current position should not be held simply because you are already in it. Sunk costs are sunk. Your entry price is irrelevant to the stock's future direction.

Another technique is to reframe the position. Instead of thinking "I am down 40% on this stock," think "I own $30,000 worth of this stock. Is that the best use of $30,000 in my portfolio right now?" This strips away the anchor and forces an evaluation based on current opportunity cost.

## What the Agent Should Know

The agent must evaluate every position based on current valuation, momentum, and fundamentals — never on historical prices or entry points. When analysing a position, the question is always "what is the risk-reward from here?" not "how far is it from where I bought it?" Anchoring is the most common bias in trading because it feels like rational analysis. It is not. The market is forward-looking. Your entry price is backward-looking. They have nothing to do with each other.
