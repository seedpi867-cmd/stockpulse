# Loss Aversion: Why You Hold Losers and Sell Winners

## The Science

Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky demonstrated in their 1979 Prospect Theory paper that losses are psychologically roughly twice as painful as equivalent gains are pleasurable. Losing $100 feels about as bad as gaining $200 feels good. This asymmetry — loss aversion — is one of the most robustly documented findings in behavioural economics and it systematically destroys trading performance.

## The Disposition Effect

Loss aversion in trading produces what Hersh Shefrin and Meir Statman (1985) called the "disposition effect": the tendency to sell winners too early and hold losers too long. It is the single most common and most expensive behavioural error in investing.

Selling winners too early: you bought a stock at $50, it goes to $70. You feel the pleasure of a $20 gain and the anxiety that it might reverse. Loss aversion makes the potential loss of that $20 gain feel twice as bad as the potential gain of another $20 on top. So you sell, locking in the $20 gain. The stock then runs to $120. You captured a small piece of a large move because the pain of potentially losing your gains outweighed the opportunity of letting them run.

Holding losers too long: you bought at $50, it drops to $35. Selling would crystallise a $15 loss, which feels terrible. Holding keeps the loss "theoretical" — as long as you have not sold, you have not really lost. So you hold, hoping for recovery. The stock drops to $20. Now the loss is $30, but selling still means accepting reality, so you hold more. This can continue to zero.

The net result: a portfolio of small wins and large losses. This is the inverse of what profitable trading requires, which is small losses and large wins.

## Why Stop Losses Work Psychologically

A stop loss is not primarily a risk management tool — it is a loss aversion override. By defining the acceptable loss before entering the trade, you make the sell decision when you are emotionally neutral. When the stop is hit, the decision is already made. You are executing a plan, not making an emotional choice in the moment.

Without a stop loss, every tick lower requires a new decision: "Should I sell now?" Loss aversion answers "no" every time until the loss is so large it triggers panic selling at the worst possible moment. The stop loss removes loss aversion from the equation.

## The Break-Even Obsession

Loss aversion creates an obsessive fixation on getting back to break-even. A position down 30% becomes an anchor point. The trader will hold through terrible news, ignore deteriorating fundamentals, and refuse to allocate capital elsewhere — all to avoid the psychological pain of booking the loss. The opportunity cost of capital trapped in "waiting to get back to even" positions is enormous and invisible.

Mathematically, a 30% loss requires a 43% gain just to return to break-even. A 50% loss requires a 100% gain. The deeper the hole, the more improbable the recovery, but loss aversion keeps you digging.

## The Professional Approach

Professional traders reframe losses as costs of doing business, like rent or payroll. A surgeon does not feel emotionally devastated when they use a scalpel — it is a tool of the trade. Similarly, a small loss is the cost of being wrong about a trade, which happens to every trader on a significant percentage of trades. The goal is not to avoid losses but to keep them small relative to wins.

The expected value equation for profitable trading is: (win rate x average win) minus (loss rate x average loss) > 0. You can be wrong 60% of the time and still be profitable if your wins are 3x your losses. Loss aversion inverts this by making wins small (sold too early) and losses large (held too long).

## What the Agent Should Know

The agent must enforce position sizing and stop-loss discipline without emotional override. Every position should have a predefined exit point for both gains and losses before entry. The agent should flag positions that are being held primarily because of loss aversion — deteriorating fundamentals combined with a large unrealised loss is the signature pattern. The rational question is always: "Would I enter this position today at today's price?" If not, exit.
