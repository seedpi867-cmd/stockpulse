# Recency Bias: The Permanent State That Never Lasts

## The Trap

Whatever happened recently feels like it will continue forever. After three months of a bull market, investors believe the bull market is permanent. After a two-week crash, they believe stocks will never recover. Both beliefs are wrong every single time, and both feel absolutely certain in the moment.

Recency bias is the tendency to weight recent events far more heavily than historical base rates when forming expectations. It is hardwired into human cognition — our ancestors survived by paying attention to recent threats — but it is catastrophically bad for investing.

## How It Manifests

At market tops, recency bias produces statements like "this time is different" and "stocks only go up." Investors who experienced the 2020-2021 bull market extrapolated gains indefinitely. They levered up, bought speculative assets, and dismissed risk because recent experience taught them that dips were always bought and recovery was always swift. Then 2022 happened and the Nasdaq fell 33%.

At market bottoms, recency bias produces statements like "this is the end of capitalism" and "I will never invest again." Investors who experienced the 2008-2009 crash kept money in cash for years, missing the 400%+ rally that followed. The pain of the recent crash was so vivid that they could not believe recovery was possible.

## The Data

The American Association of Individual Investors (AAII) sentiment survey is a real-time measure of recency bias in action. Bullish sentiment peaks after sustained rallies and troughs after sustained declines — it follows prices rather than predicting them. As a contrary indicator, extreme readings in the AAII survey have a solid track record: when everyone is bullish, the market is near a top; when everyone is bearish, it is near a bottom.

Fund flow data tells the same story. Retail investors pour money into equity funds after prices have already risen (buying high) and withdraw money after prices have fallen (selling low). The Investment Company Institute publishes monthly flow data showing this pattern repeating across every market cycle going back decades.

## Why the Bias Persists

Recency bias persists because it is emotionally self-reinforcing. Seeing your portfolio go up every day creates dopamine hits that feel like evidence the strategy is working. The emotional reward of recent gains drowns out the statistical reality that all trends end. Similarly, the pain of losses is so visceral that it overrides the historical data showing that markets always recover.

Media amplification makes it worse. During bull markets, financial media runs bullish stories because they get clicks. During crashes, bearish stories dominate. The information environment is recency-biased itself, creating an echo chamber that reinforces whatever is already happening.

## The Professional Antidote

Systematic rules override recency bias. A defined allocation model, rebalancing schedule, or trend-following system does not care about how you feel. It follows data, not emotion. This is why quantitative strategies outperform discretionary ones on a risk-adjusted basis over long periods — they are immune to recency bias.

Another tool: study market history. Every crash felt like the end of the world at the time. Every bubble felt like a new paradigm. None of them were. The base rate for market recovery after a 20%+ decline is 100% — it has always recovered. The base rate for bull markets ending is also 100% — they always end. Knowing the base rates insulates against the recency trap.

## What the Agent Should Know

The agent should weight base rates and historical patterns more heavily than recent price action when forming market outlook. Recent trends inform, but they do not predict. Mean reversion is the dominant force over long periods. When sentiment is extreme in either direction, the base rate favours the contrarian position.
