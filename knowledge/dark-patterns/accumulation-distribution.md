# Accumulation and Distribution Patterns

## What Institutional Accumulation Looks Like

When large institutions (mutual funds, pension funds, hedge funds) want to build a position in a stock, they cannot simply buy millions of shares at once — it would move the price against them. Instead, they accumulate over days or weeks using algorithms designed to minimize market impact. Recognizing this accumulation before the breakout is one of the highest-edge skills in trading.

## Signs of Accumulation

**Volume pattern:** The most reliable signal is increasing volume on up days and decreasing volume on down days within a trading range. If a stock trades between 50 and 55 dollars for three weeks, and the up days consistently show 2x average volume while the down days show 0.5x average volume, institutions are buying dips and not selling rips. This creates a volume-weighted bias that precedes an upside breakout.

**On-Balance Volume (OBV) divergence:** OBV adds volume on up days and subtracts it on down days, creating a cumulative total. When price is flat or slightly declining but OBV is rising, it signals net accumulation. This divergence often resolves with a price breakout to the upside within 1-3 weeks.

**Accumulation/Distribution Line:** Marc Chaikin developed this indicator, which weights volume by where the close falls within the daily range. A close near the high on heavy volume contributes strongly to accumulation. When the A/D line trends up while price consolidates, smart money is building positions.

**Narrow range days on low volume:** After an initial selloff, accumulation often shows as a series of narrow-range, low-volume days. The stock stops going down despite negative sentiment. This lack of selling pressure indicates that weak hands have sold and strong hands are absorbing supply.

## Signs of Distribution

Distribution is the reverse — institutions selling a large position without crashing the price.

**Churning:** Price makes little progress despite high volume. A stock that trades 3x average volume but closes flat or slightly down is being distributed — sellers are meeting every buyer. This often occurs near market or stock highs when the narrative is most bullish.

**Climax top volume:** A parabolic price spike on massive volume (5-10x average) is often the final burst of buying as retail chases the move. The institution uses this retail demand as exit liquidity. GameStop in January 2021 showed textbook climax volume — the day it hit 483 dollars saw volume of 197 million shares versus an average of 7 million.

**Lower highs on high volume:** Each rally attempt reaches a lower peak than the last, but volume stays elevated. This shows sellers are becoming more aggressive at lower prices, overwhelming buyers.

## The Wyckoff Method

Richard Wyckoff formalized accumulation/distribution analysis in the 1930s. His four phases remain highly relevant:

1. **Selling climax (SC):** Panic selling creates a sharp low on extreme volume. This marks the beginning of the range.
2. **Automatic rally (AR):** A reflex bounce as selling exhausts. This sets the upper boundary of the range.
3. **Secondary test (ST):** Price retests the selling climax low on lower volume. Successful test (holds above SC low) confirms demand is absorbing supply.
4. **Spring:** A brief break below the trading range low on low volume that quickly reverses. This shakeout clears the last weak holders and is the highest-probability entry point. The spring is followed by a sign of strength rally that breaks above the range on expanding volume.

Recognizing a Wyckoff spring in real time is extremely valuable — it often precedes a 15-30% rally as the accumulated position drives price higher once distribution has been completed.
