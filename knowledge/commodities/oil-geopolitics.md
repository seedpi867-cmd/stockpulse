# Oil and Geopolitics: The Commodity That Starts Recessions

## The Track Record

Every sustained oil price spike above $100 per barrel (inflation-adjusted) has preceded or caused a recession. The 1973 OPEC embargo triggered the 1973-1975 recession. The 1979 Iranian revolution spike triggered the 1980-1982 recession. The 1990 Gulf War spike contributed to the 1990-1991 recession. The 2008 spike to $147 coincided with the Great Financial Crisis. Oil is not just a commodity — it is an input cost for the entire economy, and when it spikes, it acts as a tax on consumers and businesses.

## Why Oil Is Different

Oil is the most geopolitically influenced commodity in the world. Its price is determined by supply decisions made by governments, not by market forces alone. OPEC+ (the Organisation of Petroleum Exporting Countries plus Russia and other allies) controls roughly 40% of global oil production and can unilaterally cut or increase supply to target price ranges.

This makes oil prices a function of political relationships, not just economics. Saudi Arabia-US relations, Russia sanctions, Iran nuclear negotiations, Venezuelan political stability, Libyan civil conflict, Nigerian security — all directly affect the price you pay for petrol and the margin every company earns.

## OPEC+ as a Cartel

OPEC+ members meet regularly to set production quotas. When they cut production, supply falls and prices rise. When they increase production, prices fall. The cartel's cohesion is variable — members frequently cheat on their quotas, producing more than agreed to capture market share. But when they are unified (as during the 2020 COVID cuts and subsequent controlled supply increases), they can move oil prices by 30-50%.

The key dynamic: Saudi Arabia is the swing producer. Only Saudi Arabia has significant spare capacity — the ability to increase production by 1-2 million barrels per day within months. This gives Saudi Arabia enormous pricing power and makes the Saudi-US relationship a core macro input.

## US Shale as Counterweight

The shale revolution changed oil geopolitics permanently. US oil production went from 5 million barrels per day in 2008 to over 13 million in 2023, making the US the world's largest producer. Shale acts as a price ceiling: when oil rises above $70-80 per barrel, shale producers are highly profitable and increase drilling, bringing more supply online within 6-12 months. This has compressed the upside range for oil prices.

However, shale has not eliminated price spikes. Geopolitical supply disruptions can still overwhelm the shale response because shale takes months to ramp while a pipeline attack or shipping strait blockade removes supply instantly.

## Oil as Real-Time Economic Activity

Beyond geopolitics, oil demand is a real-time indicator of economic activity. Every truck, ship, plane, and factory consumes oil. When oil demand falls — visible in weekly inventory reports (EIA crude oil inventory data, released every Wednesday at 10:30 AM EST) — economic activity is contracting. When inventories build unexpectedly, demand is weaker than expected. When inventories draw down, demand is stronger.

The crack spread — the difference between crude oil prices and refined product prices (gasoline, diesel) — indicates refinery profitability and downstream demand. A widening crack spread means strong consumer demand for fuel. A narrowing crack spread means demand is weakening.

## Sanctions and Supply Risk

Oil sanctions are a primary tool of US foreign policy. Sanctions on Iran, Russia, and Venezuela have removed millions of barrels per day from the global market (or redirected them through grey markets at discounted prices). Changes in sanctions policy — tightening or easing — directly affect global supply and prices.

The Russia-Ukraine conflict demonstrated how energy weaponisation works in practice. Russia cut gas supplies to Europe, Europe sanctioned Russian oil, Russia redirected exports to India and China at discounts, and global energy prices spiked to levels that triggered inflation and central bank tightening worldwide.

## What the Agent Should Know

Oil is both an economic indicator and a geopolitical weapon. Track WTI and Brent crude prices, EIA weekly inventory data, OPEC+ production decisions, and geopolitical developments in key producing regions. Oil above $100 for sustained periods is recessionary. Oil below $60 is stimulative. Oil demand data (inventories, crack spreads) provides real-time economic intelligence. Any position in the equity market is implicitly a position on oil — energy costs affect every company's margins.
