# The Dot-Com Bubble (1999-2002)

## The Mania

The dot-com bubble was the most spectacular speculative mania since the 1920s. The NASDAQ Composite rose from 1,291 in January 1997 to 5,048 on March 10, 2000 — a 291% gain in just over three years. Companies with no revenue, no business model, and little more than a website and a ".com" suffix attracted billion-dollar valuations.

Key examples of peak absurdity: Pets.com had a $300 million market cap and went bankrupt within a year. Webvan raised $375 million in its IPO for an online grocery delivery service that burned through all of it in 18 months. Theglobe.com surged 606% on its first day of trading — the largest first-day IPO gain at the time — then eventually traded below $0.10. eToys had a higher market cap ($8 billion) than Toys R Us ($6 billion) despite having 1/100th the revenue.

## What Made It Possible

**Easy money:** The Fed kept rates low after the 1998 Long-Term Capital Management crisis and the Asian financial crisis, flooding the system with liquidity.

**New paradigm narrative:** "Eyeballs" and "clicks" replaced earnings as valuation metrics. Analysts at major banks published research justifying infinite price-to-sales ratios for internet companies. Henry Blodget of Merrill Lynch set a $400 target on Amazon when it traded at $240 — it hit $400 within weeks, cementing his reputation.

**Retail frenzy:** Online brokerages (E*Trade, Ameritrade) made trading accessible to millions for the first time. Day trading became a cultural phenomenon. Books like "Trading for a Living" topped bestseller lists.

## The Crash

The NASDAQ peaked on March 10, 2000 at 5,048. By October 2002, it had fallen to 1,114 — a 78% decline. The broader S&P 500 fell 49% peak to trough.

Major casualties: Cisco fell from $80 to $11, losing $400 billion in market cap. Intel dropped from $75 to $15. Amazon fell from $107 to $7 — but survived and ultimately vindicated its model. Sun Microsystems went from $64 to $2.83. JDS Uniphase wrote off $50 billion in goodwill — the largest corporate writedown in history at the time.

The decline was slow and grinding, unlike the sharp crashes of 1987 or 2020. There were multiple bear market rallies of 20-40% that trapped buyers (notably the rally from March to May 2001). The final bottom in October 2002 coincided with the onset of the Iraq War drums and the WorldCom and Enron accounting scandals destroying what little trust remained.

## Lessons

**Valuation matters eventually.** Companies trading at 50-100x revenue with no path to profitability are not investments — they are lottery tickets. The survivors (Amazon, eBay, Priceline) were companies with real business models; the hundreds that went to zero had none.

**Bear market rallies are traps.** The NASDAQ rallied 40% from May to July 2000, convincing many the bottom was in. It then fell another 65%. In prolonged bear markets, the risk of catching a falling knife is higher than in V-shaped recoveries.

**The NASDAQ did not recover its March 2000 high until April 2015** — fifteen years. An entire generation of investors who bought the top waited over a decade just to break even. Time horizon and position sizing are existential concerns, not theoretical ones.
