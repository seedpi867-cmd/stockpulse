# The 2008 Global Financial Crisis

## Timeline of Collapse

The 2008 crisis was the worst financial meltdown since the Great Depression. It wiped out $17 trillion in US household wealth, caused 8.7 million job losses, and fundamentally changed financial regulation.

**2007 Precursors:** Subprime mortgage delinquencies began rising in late 2006. By February 2007, HSBC wrote down $10.5 billion in subprime losses — the first major warning. In June 2007, two Bear Stearns hedge funds heavily invested in CDOs collapsed. The ABX index (tracking subprime mortgage bonds) began its decline. Most equity investors ignored these signals; the S&P 500 made its all-time high of 1,565 on October 9, 2007.

**Bear Stearns (March 2008):** A run on Bear Stearns forced a Fed-brokered sale to JPMorgan at $2/share (later raised to $10), down from $170 eighteen months prior. The Fed backstopped $30 billion in toxic assets. Markets rallied briefly, believing the worst was contained.

**Lehman Brothers (September 15, 2008):** The pivotal moment. When the Fed and Treasury allowed Lehman to file for bankruptcy (the largest in US history at $639 billion in assets), the commercial paper market froze overnight. Money market funds "broke the buck" — the Reserve Primary Fund fell below $1 NAV due to Lehman exposure, triggering $300 billion in money market redemptions within a week.

**Cascade (September-October 2008):** AIG required an $85 billion government bailout (eventually $182 billion). Washington Mutual became the largest bank failure in US history. The S&P 500 fell 28.5% in October 2008 alone. The VIX reached 89.53. Interbank lending effectively stopped — LIBOR-OIS spreads (a measure of banking system stress) hit 364 basis points, versus a normal reading of 10.

**TARP and Bottom (October 2008-March 2009):** The $700 billion Troubled Asset Relief Program (TARP) was signed October 3, 2008. Markets initially crashed another 25% after TARP passed. The S&P 500 bottomed at 666 on March 9, 2009 — down 57% from the October 2007 peak. The bottom came with Citigroup reporting profitable months and the Fed announcing QE1 ($1.75 trillion in asset purchases).

## Key Lessons for Traders

**Credit markets lead equities.** The ABX index and credit spreads deteriorated months before the stock market peaked. Always monitor investment-grade and high-yield credit spreads (ICE BofA indices) as leading indicators.

**"Contained" never is.** Every official statement from February 2007 through August 2008 described subprime as "contained" and "well-managed." When authorities need to reassure markets about contagion, contagion is already happening.

**Liquidity vanishes when you need it most.** Bid-ask spreads on even blue-chip stocks widened to 1-3% during October 2008. Limit orders that would normally fill in seconds sat unfilled for minutes. Market orders faced severe slippage. Position sizing must account for the possibility that you cannot exit at your stop price.

**The bottom is always ugly.** In March 2009, unemployment was still rising (it peaked at 10% in October 2009), housing was still falling, and no major macro indicator had turned positive. Bottoms are made on exhaustion of selling, not improvement in fundamentals.
