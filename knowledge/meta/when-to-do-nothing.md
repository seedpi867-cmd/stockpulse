# When to Do Nothing — The Hardest and Most Profitable Skill

## The Problem

Doing nothing feels wrong. Every part of human psychology pushes toward action:

- **Loss aversion in reverse.** Not being in a position feels like missing out. The unrealized gains of a trade you did not take feel like real losses, even though they are not.
- **Activity bias.** We equate effort with results. Sitting in cash feels lazy. Placing trades feels productive. But in trading, the relationship between activity and results is often inverse.
- **Boredom.** Markets are open 6.5 hours a day, 252 days a year. Watching prices move without participating is psychologically difficult. The temptation to trade "just to be doing something" is constant.
- **Social pressure.** Other traders are discussing their positions. Financial media assumes you are positioned. Not having a view feels like not being prepared. But not having a view IS a view — the view that the current setup does not justify risking capital.

## The Math of Overtrading

Consider a trader with a genuine edge: 55% win rate, 1.5:1 reward-to-risk. This is a profitable strategy IF applied selectively to high-quality setups.

If this trader takes 10 trades per month on only the best setups, the edge compounds. If the same trader, driven by boredom or FOMO, takes 40 trades per month, the marginal 30 trades are on weaker setups — maybe 50% win rate, 1.2:1 reward-to-risk. These marginal trades are at best break-even and at worst losing propositions. They dilute the edge from the 10 good trades and add transaction costs.

The paradox: trading MORE with a real edge can make you LESS profitable because the additional trades are lower quality.

## When the Agent Should Do Nothing

### 1. No Clear Regime
When the regime detection system (see volatility-regimes.md) cannot confidently classify the current environment, the correct response is to wait. Deploying a trending strategy in what might be a range-bound market, or a mean-reversion strategy in what might be a trend, is gambling with a coin flip that charges commission.

### 2. Conflicting Signals
When liquidity indicators say one thing, breadth says another, and sector leadership is confused, the information set is ambiguous. Ambiguity does not mean "pick the signals I like." It means the market is in transition and the direction is genuinely unclear. Wait for signal clarity.

### 3. Low Conviction
If the agent cannot articulate a specific thesis for a trade — a clear "I am buying X because Y is happening and I expect Z" — the trade should not be taken. "It looks like it might go up" is not a thesis. "The stock is near support" is not a thesis. A thesis requires a catalyst, an expected outcome, and a time frame.

### 4. Post-Loss Emotional State
After a significant loss, the impulse to trade immediately to "make it back" is overwhelming and almost always destructive. The revenge trade is the most expensive trade in any trader's career. After a loss exceeding 2% of portfolio, mandatory cooling off: no new positions for 24 hours minimum.

### 5. High VVIX Environment
When the volatility of volatility is elevated (see volatility-of-volatility.md), risk management tools become unreliable. If you cannot accurately size positions or set stops because the market's behavior is unpredictable, do not trade. Cash is the only position with zero risk.

### 6. Pre-Major Event
Before FOMC decisions, major earnings (when positioned in the name), or known geopolitical events, the risk/reward of new positions is asymmetric in the wrong direction. The event can move markets more than any setup can predict. Wait for the event, assess the reaction, then act.

## Cash Is a Position

Holding cash earns the risk-free rate (currently meaningful at 4-5% in money market funds). More importantly, cash provides:

- **Optionality.** When a genuine opportunity appears — a crash, a dislocation, a perfect setup — cash allows you to act. The trader who is fully invested during a crash has no buying power at the best prices.
- **Psychological capital.** Not losing money preserves confidence and decision-making quality. A trader sitting in cash with a clear mind will make better decisions than a trader sitting in losing positions while trying to manage damage.
- **Survival.** The primary goal of trading is to survive long enough for the edge to compound. Every day you are in the market without an edge is a day you risk depleting capital that you will need later.

## For the Agent

Build explicit criteria for "no trade" conditions:
- Regime unclear → no new positions
- VVIX > 120 → no new positions
- Conflicting signals across 3+ indicators → no new positions
- Last loss > 2% portfolio → 24-hour freeze
- No articulable thesis → no trade

The agent should track how often it CHOOSES not to trade and compare the outcomes: what would have happened if it had traded during "do nothing" periods? Over time, this data will validate (or refine) the do-nothing criteria. The hypothesis: periods of inaction will show better risk-adjusted outcomes than periods of forced action.
