# Edge Decay and Strategy Lifecycle

## Every Edge Has a Lifespan

A trading edge is a systematic advantage that produces positive expected returns. No edge lasts forever. As more participants discover and exploit an edge, their collective trading eliminates the inefficiency that created it. Understanding the lifecycle of edges — how they emerge, peak, and die — is arguably the most important meta-skill in trading.

## The Edge Lifecycle

**Discovery Phase:** A trader or researcher identifies a market inefficiency. Perhaps small-cap value stocks consistently outperform after earnings surprises, or stocks with high short interest squeeze more violently during market rallies. At this stage, few participants exploit the edge and returns are highest.

**Early Adoption:** Word spreads through academic papers, trading books, or fund performance. A handful of sophisticated funds begin systematically trading the edge. Returns remain strong but begin to compress as more capital competes for the same opportunities.

**Crowding:** The edge becomes widely known. ETFs and factor funds are created to capture it (momentum ETFs, value ETFs, low-volatility ETFs). Retail traders learn about it through social media and courses. The edge is now crowded — too much capital chases too little alpha. Returns compress to near-zero or become negative when the crowded trade unwinds.

**Decay or Death:** The edge either disappears entirely (the inefficiency is arbitraged away) or inverts (the crowded trade creates a new inefficiency in the opposite direction). The momentum factor premium, for example, has declined from roughly 8% annually pre-2000 to roughly 2-4% post-2000 as momentum ETFs and quant funds proliferated.

## Examples of Edge Decay

**January Effect:** Small-cap stocks historically outperformed in January, driven by tax-loss selling in December and reinvestment in January. The effect was documented in the 1980s and was robust going back to the 1920s. After publication, it steadily weakened. By the 2000s, the January effect had largely disappeared as traders front-ran the seasonal pattern in December.

**Post-Earnings Drift:** Still exists but has compressed significantly since its documentation in 1989. The drift that once persisted for 60-90 days now compresses into 20-30 days as algorithmic traders exploit it faster.

**Closed-End Fund Discounts:** Closed-end funds once traded at persistent discounts to NAV (net asset value). Buying at a 15% discount and waiting for mean reversion was a reliable edge. As arbitrage funds and activist investors targeted these discounts, the average discount narrowed from 10-15% historically to 3-5%.

## Defending Against Edge Decay

Continuously monitor the Sharpe ratio and win rate of every strategy you run on a rolling 6-month and 12-month basis. If a strategy that historically produced a Sharpe of 1.2 has declined to 0.5 over the past year, the edge may be decaying. The temptation is to increase leverage to compensate for lower returns per unit of capital — this is exactly the wrong response and the path to blowup.

Diversify across multiple independent edges rather than concentrating in one. When one edge decays, others may still be performing. Allocate research time to discovering new edges, not just trading existing ones. The traders who survive decades are the ones who continuously adapt, not the ones who find one great strategy and ride it forever.

The best edges are the ones that are hardest to scale, require unique data, or exploit behavioral biases that are hardwired into human psychology (loss aversion, anchoring, herding). These decay more slowly because they cannot be easily arbitraged by capital alone.
