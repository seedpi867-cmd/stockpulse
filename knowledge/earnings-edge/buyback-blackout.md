# Buyback Blackout Periods — When the Biggest Buyer Disappears

## The Mechanism

Publicly traded companies that have authorized share buyback programs are subject to self-imposed (and sometimes legally required) blackout periods around earnings announcements. During these periods, the company cannot repurchase its own shares in the open market.

The typical blackout window: begins approximately 2-4 weeks before the earnings announcement date and extends 2 business days after the announcement (48 hours after results are public). Some companies have longer blackouts — up to 5 weeks before. The exact timing varies by company policy, but SEC Rule 10b-18 provides the safe harbor framework.

## Why It Matters

Share buybacks are an enormous source of demand for US equities. In recent years, S&P 500 companies have collectively repurchased $800 billion to over $1 trillion in shares annually. For some individual companies, buybacks represent the single largest source of buying pressure. Apple, for example, has spent over $600 billion on buybacks since 2012 — they are their own largest buyer by far.

When this buying disappears during blackout periods, it removes a significant mechanical bid from the market. The effect is measurable:

- **Goldman Sachs research** has documented that S&P 500 stocks underperform during peak blackout windows (when the most companies are simultaneously in blackout) by an average of 0.5-1.0% compared to non-blackout periods.
- **Volume patterns shift.** Buyback-driven demand typically occurs in the last 30 minutes of trading (companies often execute buybacks near the close to minimize market impact). During blackouts, this end-of-day bid disappears.
- **Volatility increases.** With a steady buyer removed, price swings tend to be wider during blackout periods.

## The Calendar Effect

Because most S&P 500 companies report earnings within a concentrated 4-6 week window each quarter, blackout periods overlap significantly. The peak blackout window — when the most companies are simultaneously restricted — typically falls in the 2-3 weeks before earnings season kicks off.

Key timing:
- **Q1 earnings (April):** Blackout peaks late March to mid-April
- **Q2 earnings (July):** Blackout peaks late June to mid-July
- **Q3 earnings (October):** Blackout peaks late September to mid-October
- **Q4 earnings (January):** Blackout peaks late December to mid-January

Goldman Sachs publishes a "Buyback Blackout" indicator showing the percentage of S&P 500 companies currently in blackout. When this exceeds 80%, the mechanical headwind is strongest.

## The Blackout Lift

Equally important: when the blackout lifts after earnings, buybacks resume. Companies that just reported strong results and reaffirmed their buyback programs return to the market as active buyers. This creates a mechanical bid that supports prices for the 2-3 months following earnings.

The lift effect is strongest in the first two weeks after blackout ends, when companies that have been restricted for a month rush to execute accumulated buyback capacity.

## Practical Application

For the agent:
- Track the aggregate buyback blackout calendar. During peak blackout (80%+ of S&P 500 restricted), expect weaker markets and wider volatility.
- Avoid initiating large long positions during peak blackout unless the setup is exceptionally strong — you are buying without the corporate buyback safety net.
- After blackout lifts, stocks with active large buyback programs will have a tailwind. This is especially powerful for mega-cap tech (Apple, Alphabet, Meta, Microsoft) where buyback programs are massive relative to daily volume.
- Watch for companies that announce buyback program INCREASES alongside earnings — when the blackout lifts, these companies will be even more aggressive buyers.
