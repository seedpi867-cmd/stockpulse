# Whisper Numbers — The Expectation Behind the Expectation

## What They Are

The "consensus estimate" you see on Yahoo Finance or Bloomberg is the average of published analyst forecasts. The whisper number is what traders, buy-side analysts, and institutional investors ACTUALLY expect — the unofficial, unwritten bar that a company must clear to avoid a negative reaction.

Whisper numbers are almost always higher than consensus. Why? Analyst estimates are anchored by company guidance (which is deliberately conservative) and herd behavior (nobody wants to be the outlier). The buy-side, talking to industry contacts, running proprietary models, and pricing in momentum, arrives at a higher number. That higher number is the real expectation priced into the stock.

## Why Consensus Beats Can Still Drop

This is one of the most confusing experiences for new traders: a company beats consensus earnings by 5%, and the stock drops 3%. The explanation is almost always the whisper number. The company beat the published bar but missed the real one.

Example: consensus expects $1.50 EPS. The whisper is $1.60. Company reports $1.55. Headlines read "beats estimates." The stock drops because $1.55 was a miss relative to what was actually priced in.

## Where to Find Whisper Numbers

- **Estimize** (estimize.com) — crowdsourced estimates from buy-side analysts, independent researchers, and sophisticated individual investors. Their consensus tends to be more accurate than Wall Street consensus because it incorporates a broader, less conflicted sample.
- **WhisperNumber.com** — one of the original whisper number aggregators, tracks the gap between whisper and consensus.
- **Options-implied expectations** — the options market prices in an expected move around earnings. If options imply a 5% move and the stock typically moves 3% on earnings, the market is expecting something bigger than consensus suggests.
- **Buy-side channel checks** — institutional investors talk to suppliers, customers, and competitors to build their own estimates. You cannot access this directly, but Estimize captures some of it.

## How to Use This

1. **Before earnings, compare consensus vs whisper.** If the gap is large (whisper 10%+ above consensus), the stock is priced for a big beat. Even a solid beat might disappoint.
2. **Track whisper accuracy by company.** Some companies consistently beat whispers (strong execution, conservative guidance culture). Others consistently miss. The pattern matters.
3. **Combine with options-implied move.** If the options market implies a 7% move but historical earnings moves average 4%, the options market is pricing in whisper-level expectations. Selling premium might be the trade if you think expectations are too high.
4. **Post-earnings, compare actual vs whisper, not actual vs consensus.** The stock reaction will make more sense when measured against the right bar.

## The Meta-Game

Companies know about whisper numbers. CFOs manage expectations by guiding low, knowing analysts will publish conservative numbers, so the company can "beat." This is a game of expectations management, not business performance. The best-performing stocks are those that consistently beat both consensus AND whisper — companies where execution genuinely outpaces even elevated expectations.

The agent should maintain a whisper vs consensus vs actual database for tracked stocks. Patterns in this data reveal which companies are genuinely outperforming and which are just playing the guidance game.
