# Guidance vs Beat — The Market Looks Forward, Not Backward

## The Fundamental Truth

Earnings beats are backward-looking. Guidance is forward-looking. The market is a discounting mechanism that prices in the future, not the past. When a company reports earnings, the number itself is already priced in (or close to it). What is NOT priced in is management's updated view of the future.

This is why the single most important line in any earnings call is not "we earned $X" but "we expect next quarter/year to be..."

## The Four Quadrants

Every earnings report falls into one of four categories:

1. **Beat + Raise guidance:** The golden combination. Stock rallies hard. Past was better than expected AND future is brighter. This is where multi-day earnings drift is strongest.

2. **Beat + Lower guidance:** The trap. Headlines scream "beats estimates!" but the stock drops. The past was fine but the future is deteriorating. This is where most retail traders get burned — they buy the beat, not realizing the guide-down is the real signal.

3. **Miss + Raise guidance:** The contrarian setup. Stock might dip initially on the miss, then recover or rally as investors digest the improved outlook. The miss might be due to one-time items, while the raised guidance reflects genuine business improvement.

4. **Miss + Lower guidance:** The disaster. Stock craters. Past was bad AND future is worse. The earnings drift here is most severe — do not try to catch the falling knife.

## What "Guidance" Actually Means

Companies provide guidance in different forms:
- **Explicit EPS/revenue ranges** — most useful. "We expect Q3 revenue of $4.2-4.4 billion"
- **Qualitative commentary** — less useful but still informative. "We see strengthening demand in our enterprise segment"
- **Guidance withdrawal** — a strong negative signal. When a company pulls guidance entirely, it means they cannot forecast their own business. This happened widely during COVID and is always bearish.
- **Long-term target updates** — updating 3-year revenue targets up or down signals structural changes

## The Guidance Game

Management teams are sophisticated about guidance. Most guide conservatively — setting a low bar they know they can clear. This creates a cycle: guide low, beat, stock rises, repeat. The "beat" is manufactured.

The edge is identifying when guidance breaks the pattern:
- **A company that usually guides conservatively suddenly guides aggressively** — management is so confident they are willing to raise the bar. Very bullish.
- **A company that usually guides in-line suddenly guides down** — something is genuinely wrong, not just sandbagging. Very bearish.
- **The magnitude of the guide vs consensus for the guided period** — if consensus for next quarter is $1.50 and the company guides $1.60-1.70, that is a meaningful raise. If they guide $1.45-1.55, it is a meaningful cut even if current quarter beat.

## Practical Application

For the agent:
- Always compare guidance midpoint to consensus for the guided period, not just whether guidance was "raised" or "lowered" vs the company's own prior guidance
- Track guidance revision history by company — build a database of guidance accuracy (do they consistently under-promise and over-deliver?)
- Weight guidance more heavily than the beat/miss for position decisions
- When a stock drops on a beat, immediately check guidance — the guide-down explains the reaction almost every time
- When a stock rallies on a miss, check guidance — the raise explains the rally
