# MACD — Moving Average Convergence Divergence

## What It Is
MACD measures the relationship between two exponential moving averages. Created by Gerald Appel in the late 1970s. It is both a trend-following and momentum indicator. The standard settings are 12-period EMA minus 26-period EMA, with a 9-period EMA signal line.

## Components
- **MACD Line:** 12 EMA minus 26 EMA. When the faster EMA is above the slower, MACD is positive (bullish momentum). When below, negative (bearish).
- **Signal Line:** 9-period EMA of the MACD line. Acts as a trigger for buy/sell signals.
- **Histogram:** Visual representation of the distance between MACD line and signal line. When the histogram is growing, momentum is accelerating. When shrinking, momentum is decelerating — even if still positive.

## Core Signals

**Signal Line Crossover:** The most basic signal. MACD crosses above the signal line = bullish. MACD crosses below = bearish. In strong trends, these crossovers work well. In choppy markets, they generate painful whipsaws.

**Zero Line Crossover:** MACD crossing above zero means the 12 EMA has crossed above the 26 EMA — a more significant event than signal line crossovers. This confirms a shift in the intermediate trend. Crossing below zero confirms bearish trend change.

**Histogram Reversal:** When the histogram starts shrinking (even while still positive), momentum is fading. This often precedes the actual MACD/signal crossover by several bars, giving an early warning. Histogram crossing from positive to negative confirms the bearish crossover.

## Divergences

**Bullish Divergence:** Price makes lower lows but MACD makes higher lows. Selling pressure is losing steam. This is most powerful when it occurs near a significant support level.

**Bearish Divergence:** Price makes higher highs but MACD makes lower highs. Buying momentum is weakening despite new highs. This warned of the 2007 top, the 2000 top, and many others.

**Important caveat:** Divergences can persist for a long time before price actually reverses. Use them as alerts, not immediate triggers. Wait for price confirmation — a break of support/resistance or a candlestick reversal pattern.

## False Signals and How to Filter Them
1. **Choppy, range-bound markets** generate constant MACD crossovers that go nowhere. Solution: check if the market is trending first (use ADX or visually). If ADX is below 20, ignore MACD crossovers.
2. **Lagging nature:** MACD is built on moving averages, which are inherently lagging. By the time MACD gives a signal, a significant portion of the move may be over. Accept this — MACD catches the meat of moves, not the exact top or bottom.
3. **Low-volatility environments** produce tiny MACD values that cross back and forth with no conviction. Look at the histogram size — if the bars are barely visible, the signal has no power.

## Settings
- **Default (12, 26, 9):** Works well on daily charts for swing trading and position trading.
- **Fast (5, 13, 1):** More responsive, more signals, more noise. Day traders sometimes use this.
- **Slow (19, 39, 9):** Fewer signals, higher quality. Good for weekly charts or longer-term position trading.

## Combining MACD With Other Indicators
- **MACD + RSI:** MACD gives the trend direction, RSI gives overbought/oversold timing within that trend. MACD bullish + RSI oversold = strong buy setup.
- **MACD + Volume:** A MACD signal line crossover accompanied by above-average volume is far more reliable than one on thin volume.
- **MACD + Price Action:** Always check what price is doing at key levels. A bullish MACD crossover at major support is much more meaningful than one in the middle of nowhere.

## Key Takeaway
MACD tells you where momentum is heading, not where price will go. Use it to confirm trends and spot momentum shifts, but always combine with price structure and volume.
