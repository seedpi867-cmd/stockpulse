# The Dollar Milkshake Theory: The Endgame for Global Debt

## The Theory

Brent Johnson, CEO of Santiago Capital, developed the Dollar Milkshake Theory to explain why the US dollar will strengthen even as the US itself deteriorates fiscally. The core insight is that the global financial system is denominated in dollars, and there is far more dollar-denominated debt in the world than there are dollars to service it. This structural mismatch creates forced demand for dollars that overwhelms everything else.

## The Mechanism

Think of the global economy as a giant milkshake, with all the world's assets and liquidity pooled together. The US dollar — as the world's reserve currency — is the biggest straw in the glass. When global liquidity contracts (as central banks tighten, trade slows, or crises erupt), the dollar straw sucks up more than its fair share.

Here is why: roughly $13 trillion in dollar-denominated debt exists outside the United States. When a Brazilian company borrows in dollars, it must earn dollars (or convert reais to dollars) to make interest payments. When a Turkish bank issues dollar bonds, it needs dollar revenue to service them. This creates constant demand for dollars that is completely independent of the US economy's health.

When times are good and global liquidity is abundant, this demand is easily met. When liquidity tightens, every debtor scrambles for dollars simultaneously. The dollar strengthens because it is needed, not because it is fundamentally sound.

## The Cascade

A strengthening dollar triggers a vicious cascade in emerging markets and commodity-producing nations. Their dollar-denominated debts become more expensive to service in local currency terms. Their commodity exports (priced in dollars) buy fewer dollars worth of debt repayment. Capital flees to the safety of US assets, further strengthening the dollar. Central banks burn through dollar reserves trying to defend their currencies. Eventually, defaults and currency crises erupt.

This is not theoretical — it happened in the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis, the 2013 Taper Tantrum, the 2018 emerging market selloff, and partially in 2022 when the dollar index hit 20-year highs while emerging market currencies collapsed.

## The Endgame

Johnson argues the dollar will be the last major currency standing — the cleanest dirty shirt in the laundry. It will strengthen until it breaks something so large that the system itself is restructured. The US benefits temporarily (capital flows in, Treasury yields stay manageable) but ultimately suffers because a strong dollar crushes US multinational earnings, makes US exports uncompetitive, and eventually forces a crisis resolution (new Bretton Woods, dollar devaluation, or a global debt restructuring).

## Investment Implications

When the dollar is strengthening: underweight emerging markets, underweight commodities, underweight international equities. US large caps with domestic revenue benefit. Treasuries benefit from flight-to-safety flows.

When the dollar is weakening: the entire trade reverses. Emerging markets, commodities, gold, international equities, and small caps outperform.

The DXY (Dollar Index) is the primary tracking instrument. Watch the 100 level — above 100 the dollar is strong, below 100 the pressure on the rest of the world eases.

## What the Agent Should Know

The Dollar Milkshake is a structural theory about the architecture of global finance, not a short-term trading signal. It provides the framework for understanding why dollar strength persists despite US deficits and debt. When global stress rises, the default trade is long dollars — not because the US is strong but because everyone else needs dollars more than the US needs them. This theory has been one of the most accurate macro frameworks since 2018.
