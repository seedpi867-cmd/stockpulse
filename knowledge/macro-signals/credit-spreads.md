# Credit Spreads: The Market's Fear Gauge for the Real Economy

## What Credit Spreads Measure

A credit spread is the difference in yield between a corporate bond and a risk-free Treasury bond of the same maturity. When you buy a corporate bond instead of a Treasury, you demand extra yield to compensate for the risk that the company might default. That extra yield is the spread.

Investment-grade spreads (ICE BofA US Corporate Index, OAS) normally run 80-150 basis points. High-yield spreads (ICE BofA US High Yield Index, OAS) normally run 300-500 basis points. When spreads are at the low end of these ranges, the credit market is relaxed and risk appetite is high. When spreads widen beyond these ranges, fear is entering the market.

## Why Spreads Lead Stocks

Credit markets are dominated by institutional investors — insurance companies, pension funds, and bond fund managers — who tend to be more analytically rigorous and less emotionally driven than equity market participants. When these institutions start demanding higher yields for corporate debt, they are saying "we think default risk is rising."

Credit spreads often widen before stocks fall because bond investors are structurally more conservative. A bond investor can only earn their coupon — they have no upside beyond getting their money back. This makes them hyper-sensitive to deterioration. They start demanding wider spreads at the first sign of trouble, while equity investors are still riding momentum.

The 2007-2008 financial crisis illustrates this perfectly. High-yield spreads began widening in June 2007, expanding from 250 basis points to 500 basis points by August 2007. The stock market did not peak until October 2007. By the time Lehman failed in September 2008, high-yield spreads had already blown out to 1500 basis points. The bond market was screaming for a full year before equity investors accepted reality.

## The Key Levels

For investment-grade spreads (OAS): below 100 basis points is euphoria — everything is priced for perfection. 100-150 is healthy. 150-200 is caution. Above 200 is stress. Above 300 is crisis.

For high-yield spreads: below 350 is euphoria. 350-500 is healthy. 500-700 is caution. Above 700 is stress. Above 1000 is crisis — default rates are about to spike.

## The CCC Spread as a Canary

Within high yield, the lowest-quality CCC-rated bonds are the most sensitive to economic deterioration. The spread between CCC bonds and BB bonds (the highest quality junk) is a canary within the canary. When CCC spreads widen while BB spreads stay tight, the market is differentiating between companies that can survive a downturn and those that cannot. This divergence precedes broader high-yield spread widening.

## Credit Spread Compression as a Buy Signal

The reverse is equally useful. When credit spreads are very wide and begin compressing, it signals that the worst is over. Institutional capital is flowing back into corporate bonds, accepting less compensation for default risk. This typically occurs before the stock market bottoms. Buying equities when high-yield spreads are above 800 basis points and starting to compress has historically been one of the highest-conviction long entries.

## What the Agent Should Know

Monitor investment-grade and high-yield OAS daily (FRED provides free data). Credit spreads widening while stocks are still rising is a divergence that resolves in favour of the bond market nearly every time. Spreads compressing from wide levels is a powerful buy signal. The credit market knows before the stock market — always check spreads before making equity decisions.
