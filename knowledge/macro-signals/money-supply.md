# Money Supply (M2): The Tide That Lifts and Sinks All Boats

## The Core Relationship

M2 money supply — which includes cash, checking deposits, savings deposits, money market funds, and small time deposits — is the broadest commonly tracked measure of money in the economy. The relationship between M2 growth and asset prices is one of the most powerful macro signals that exists, operating with a lag of roughly 6-12 months.

Milton Friedman's famous dictum that "inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon" extends to asset prices. When money supply grows rapidly, that money flows into financial assets and pushes prices up. When money supply contracts or growth slows sharply, asset prices eventually follow downward.

## The 2020-2022 Case Study

This relationship played out with textbook clarity in the COVID cycle. M2 grew at 25-27% year-over-year in 2020-2021 — the fastest money supply growth since World War II. The Fed was printing money (quantitative easing) and the Treasury was distributing fiscal stimulus cheques. This money flooded into stocks, crypto, housing, and speculative assets of every kind.

In 2022, M2 growth turned negative for the first time since records began in 1959. The Fed was doing quantitative tightening (destroying money by letting bonds roll off its balance sheet) and rate hikes were discouraging lending (which creates money). The stock market peaked in January 2022 and fell 25%. Crypto crashed 75%. Housing froze. The lag was roughly 6-12 months from peak M2 growth to peak asset prices.

## How to Track It

M2 data is released weekly by the Federal Reserve (H.6 release) and monthly in seasonally adjusted form. The key metric is the year-over-year growth rate, not the absolute level. When M2 is growing above its long-term average (roughly 6-7% per year), liquidity conditions are supportive for risk assets. When M2 growth is below average or negative, liquidity is draining.

The Fed's balance sheet is a closely related signal. When the Fed buys bonds (QE), it creates bank reserves and expands M2. When it sells bonds or lets them mature (QT), it drains reserves and contracts M2. The Fed publishes its balance sheet weekly (H.4.1 release).

## Global M2

US M2 alone does not tell the full story. Global M2 — the combined money supply of the US, Europe, Japan, China, and the UK — provides a more complete picture of global liquidity. Markets are global and money flows across borders. A contraction in Chinese M2 can affect US asset prices through reduced global liquidity even if US M2 is stable.

Global M2 charts are available from various macro research providers. The year-over-year change in global M2, shifted forward by 10-12 months, has tracked global equity performance with striking fidelity.

## The Velocity Complication

Money supply growth only translates to economic and asset price impact if the money is being spent or invested — this is the velocity of money. If M2 grows 20% but everyone hoards the cash (as happened initially in 2020), the inflationary and asset price impact is delayed until velocity recovers.

Velocity of money (M2V) is published quarterly by the Fed. It has been in a secular decline since the late 1990s, which is why massive M2 growth has not produced proportional consumer inflation — the money goes into asset prices instead of goods prices.

## What the Agent Should Know

M2 is the tide. When the tide is rising (M2 growing above trend), own risk assets. When the tide is falling (M2 growth below trend or negative), reduce exposure. The lag is 6-12 months, which gives time to position. Track both US M2 and the Fed balance sheet as a composite liquidity indicator. Compare against global M2 for the full picture.
