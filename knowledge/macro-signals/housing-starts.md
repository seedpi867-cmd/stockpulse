# Housing Starts: The Economy's Canary

## Why Housing Leads Everything

Residential construction is the most interest-rate-sensitive sector of the economy. When the Federal Reserve raises rates, housing is the first sector to feel it. When rates fall, housing recovers first. This makes housing starts one of the most reliable leading indicators of overall economic direction, with a lead time of 12-18 months.

Edward Leamer of UCLA published a landmark paper in 2007 titled "Housing IS the Business Cycle." His research showed that residential investment declines have preceded 8 of the last 10 recessions, and in most cases the housing downturn began well before the recession was officially recognized. Housing is not just a sector — it is the leading edge of the entire economic cycle.

## The Transmission Mechanism

When housing starts decline, the impact ripples through the economy in waves. First-order effects hit homebuilders, contractors, and building material suppliers. Second-order effects hit appliance manufacturers, furniture companies, and home improvement retailers — new homes need to be filled with goods. Third-order effects hit local economies through reduced construction employment, slower property tax growth, and decreased consumer spending from the negative wealth effect as home prices soften.

A 20% decline in housing starts typically translates to a 1-2% drag on GDP over the following 12-18 months through these cascading effects. This is why housing is the canary — it is a small sector (roughly 3-5% of GDP) that punches far above its weight in economic impact.

## Lumber as the Early Warning of the Early Warning

Lumber futures are even more forward-looking than housing starts data. Builders purchase lumber before they begin construction, so lumber demand reflects future housing activity. When lumber prices spike, it means builders are ordering materials for projects in their pipeline — housing starts will rise. When lumber prices collapse, builders are not ordering — housing starts will fall.

The lumber/housing starts relationship is not perfect because lumber prices are also affected by supply (sawmill capacity, Canadian imports, tariffs), but the directional signal is strong. A sustained 30%+ decline in lumber prices has preceded every meaningful decline in housing starts over the last 30 years.

## Reading the Data

Housing starts data is released monthly by the US Census Bureau, roughly 2-3 weeks after the reference month. The data is volatile month-to-month (weather, permitting delays), so focus on the 3-month moving average rather than individual prints.

Building permits are released alongside starts and are even more forward-looking — a permit precedes a start by weeks to months. When permits decline but starts have not yet fallen, the decline in starts is coming.

Single-family starts matter more than multi-family. Single-family construction is more sensitive to mortgage rates and consumer confidence. Multi-family (apartments) is more driven by developer financing and rental market conditions.

## The Mortgage Rate Channel

The 30-year fixed mortgage rate is the mechanism connecting Fed policy to housing activity. Every 1 percentage point increase in mortgage rates reduces buying power by approximately 10%. When rates rose from 3% to 7% in 2022-2023, monthly mortgage payments on a median-priced home nearly doubled. Housing starts collapsed from 1.8 million annualised to 1.35 million.

Watch mortgage applications (released weekly by the MBA) for real-time demand signals. Purchase applications — not refinancing — tell you about actual homebuying demand.

## What the Agent Should Know

Housing starts are a high-conviction leading indicator with a proven track record across multiple business cycles. Track starts, permits, lumber prices, and mortgage applications as a composite housing dashboard. When all four are declining simultaneously, recession risk is elevated regardless of what other economic data says. Housing leads, everything else follows.
