# Catalyst Hierarchy: Not All Market Movers Are Equal

## Core Principle

When multiple signals conflict, you need a ranking system. A bullish earnings report does not override a hawkish Fed. A bearish analyst downgrade does not override a positive macro data release. Catalysts have a strict hierarchy of power, and the agent must weight them accordingly when signals collide.

## The Hierarchy (Strongest to Weakest)

### Tier 1: Central Bank Policy (Dominant)
Fed rate decisions, QE/QT announcements, forward guidance changes, emergency actions. These move trillions and set the gravitational field that all other assets orbit. When the Fed speaks, everything else is noise for 24-48 hours. A Fed pivot from hawkish to dovish can reverse a bear market. Nothing else has that power.

### Tier 2: Systemic Crisis / War
Bank failures, sovereign defaults, major military conflicts, pandemics. These override even Fed policy because they create uncertainty that monetary policy cannot immediately address. The 2008 crisis, COVID, a major geopolitical escalation — these reset all existing correlations and create new ones.

### Tier 3: Earnings Season
Aggregate earnings results set the fundamental floor or ceiling for equities. Individual company earnings matter for that stock and its sector. When 70% of companies beat estimates, it creates a rising tide. When they miss, it creates a falling tide. Earnings provide the fundamental reality check against narrative and positioning.

### Tier 4: Economic Data Releases
Jobs reports (NFP), CPI/PPI inflation data, GDP, ISM manufacturing, retail sales. These move markets for hours to days and shift the narrative around Fed policy expectations. Their power comes from how they change the probability distribution of the next Fed action.

### Tier 5: Geopolitical Events (Non-systemic)
Trade negotiations, sanctions, elections, regulatory changes. These create sector-specific moves and temporary volatility but rarely change the broad market trend unless they escalate to Tier 2. A tariff announcement moves specific sectors. An election changes policy expectations. But neither overrides the rate cycle.

### Tier 6: Analyst Upgrades/Downgrades
Individual stock movers. A Goldman upgrade can move a stock 3-5% for a day. But it does not change the fundamental trajectory. Useful for timing entries and exits on positions you already have a thesis for. Never build a thesis around an analyst call alone.

### Tier 7: Social Media / Retail Sentiment
Reddit squeezes, Twitter hype, influencer calls. These create violent short-term moves in individual names but have zero predictive power beyond 48 hours. They are noise generators, not signal generators. Useful only for identifying crowding and squeeze potential.

## How to Apply the Hierarchy

**Conflicting signals between tiers:** The higher tier always wins. If a company reports great earnings (Tier 3) but the Fed just raised rates unexpectedly (Tier 1), the stock goes down. Full stop. The Fed override is absolute.

**Conflicting signals within the same tier:** This is where nuance matters. Two Tier 4 data points conflicting (strong jobs but weak ISM) means the picture is mixed — reduce position size and wait for more data. Do not force a directional view when same-tier signals conflict.

**Catalyst decay:** Higher-tier catalysts have longer half-lives. A Fed decision influences markets for weeks to months. An earnings report matters for a quarter. An analyst upgrade is forgotten in two days. Match your holding period to the catalyst tier that supports your thesis.

## The Override Chain

Think of it as an interrupt system. A Tier 1 event interrupts and overrides everything below it. A Tier 2 event interrupts and overrides Tier 1. Lower tiers cannot override higher tiers — they can only modulate the magnitude within the direction set by the higher tier.

**In a rate-cutting cycle (Tier 1 bullish):**
- Bad earnings (Tier 3) create buying opportunities, not trend reversals
- Weak economic data (Tier 4) actually reinforces the cut cycle
- Analyst downgrades (Tier 6) are irrelevant noise

**In a rate-hiking cycle (Tier 1 bearish):**
- Great earnings (Tier 3) produce rallies that fail
- Strong economic data (Tier 4) is paradoxically bearish (more hikes coming)
- The entire character of lower-tier catalysts changes based on the Tier 1 regime

Always identify the dominant catalyst tier before trading any lower-tier signal.
