# Time-Lag Patterns: The Edge Is in the Timing, Not the Correlation

## Core Principle

Most correlations between economic signals and market outcomes are well-known. Yield curve inversions predict recessions. Housing slowdowns predict consumer weakness. Copper drops predict industrial earnings misses. None of this is secret. The edge is not knowing the correlation — everyone knows it. The edge is knowing the LAG between the signal and the outcome, and positioning during that lag while others wait for confirmation.

## The Major Lag Relationships

**Yield curve inversion → Recession:** 12-18 months. The curve inverts and pundits immediately scream recession, but stocks often rally for another 6-12 months after inversion. The recession arrives 12-18 months later. This lag is where most people get shaken out — they short at inversion, get squeezed during the late-cycle rally, then miss the actual decline.

**Housing starts decline → Consumer spending decline:** 6-9 months. When housing starts dropping, it takes two quarters for the downstream effects to hit: fewer appliance purchases, less furniture spending, fewer home improvement projects, reduced construction employment. The consumer spending data will look fine for months after housing starts rolling over.

**M2 money supply change → Asset prices:** 6-12 months. When M2 growth accelerates, asset prices follow with roughly a 6-12 month lag. When M2 contracts, asset prices deflate on the same delay. This is one of the most reliable macro signals and one of the hardest to trade because the lag is so long that it tests your conviction repeatedly.

**Copper price decline → Industrial earnings miss:** 3-6 months. Copper is "Dr. Copper" because it has a PhD in economics. When copper drops, industrial companies will miss earnings estimates one to two quarters later. The analysts revising estimates lag the copper signal by months.

**Initial jobless claims rise → Equity market peak:** 3-6 months. When claims start a sustained uptrend, the equity market has historically peaked within 3-6 months. But the initial claims data is noisy, so the signal needs to be confirmed with a 4-week moving average trend.

**ISM Manufacturing below 50 → Earnings recession:** 2-4 quarters. When ISM goes contractionary, corporate earnings growth goes negative within 2-4 quarters. The market sometimes front-runs this quickly, sometimes ignores it for a full quarter.

## How to Use Lags

**Step 1: Identify the signal.** A leading indicator has changed direction or crossed a threshold.

**Step 2: Estimate the lag.** Based on historical patterns, when does the downstream effect typically materialize? Be specific — "6-12 months" is better than "eventually."

**Step 3: Position during the lag.** This is the hard part. You have a signal that says something bad (or good) is coming, but the market has not reacted yet. Build the position gradually. Do not go all-in at the signal — use the lag period to scale in as confirming data arrives.

**Step 4: Watch for acceleration or cancelation.** Some lags get shortened when multiple signals align. If the yield curve inverts AND housing drops AND copper falls simultaneously, the recession may arrive faster than the historical average. Conversely, massive fiscal stimulus can extend or cancel a lag entirely.

## The Psychological Challenge

Trading with a lag means being early. Being early feels identical to being wrong. You will hold positions that look stupid for weeks or months before they pay off. The discipline is in the position sizing — small enough to survive the lag, large enough to matter when the thesis plays out.

## Lag Tracking System

Maintain a running log of signals and their expected lag periods. When a signal fires, note the date, the expected impact, the expected timing, and then check back. This builds your personal database of how lags actually behave versus how they should behave in theory. Over time, your lag estimates will become more precise than the textbook numbers.

The crowd knows what will happen. You know when.
