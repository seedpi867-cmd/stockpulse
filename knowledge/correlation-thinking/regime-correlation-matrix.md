# Regime-Correlation Matrix: The Rules Change Based on the Environment

## Core Principle

The biggest mistake in cross-asset analysis is applying correlations from one regime to another. In normal markets, stocks and bonds are inversely correlated — that is the basis of the 60/40 portfolio. In QE, everything goes up together. In stagflation, stocks and bonds both go down. If you apply normal-regime correlations during stagflation, every trade will fail. You must first identify the current regime, then select the correct correlation matrix.

## The Four Primary Regimes

### 1. Normal Growth (Goldilocks)
**Characteristics:** Moderate growth, low-to-moderate inflation, stable monetary policy.
**Correlation matrix:**
- Stocks up, bonds flat to slightly down (inverse)
- Dollar stable, gold flat
- Commodities follow demand (gradual)
- VIX low and stable
- Credit spreads tight

**Trading implication:** Buy dips in equities, carry trades work, sell volatility. This is the easiest environment. Standard cross-asset correlations hold.

### 2. Quantitative Easing / Liquidity Flood
**Characteristics:** Central bank buying assets, rates at zero or negative, money supply expanding rapidly.
**Correlation matrix:**
- Everything up: stocks up, bonds up, gold up, real estate up
- All correlations trend toward +1 because the driver is liquidity, not fundamentals
- Dollar weakens (more supply)
- VIX suppressed artificially
- Credit spreads collapse (everyone reaches for yield)

**Trading implication:** Buy everything. Seriously. In QE, the only wrong trade is being short or in cash. The rising tide lifts all boats. But know that this regime is artificial and will end — when it does, the correlations snap back violently.

### 3. Stagflation
**Characteristics:** Low or negative growth, high inflation, central bank paralyzed (raising rates kills growth, cutting rates feeds inflation).
**Correlation matrix:**
- Stocks down, bonds down (BOTH lose — the 60/40 portfolio breaks)
- Commodities up (inflation)
- Gold up (inflation hedge + fear)
- Dollar uncertain (depends on relative global conditions)
- VIX elevated and spiky
- Credit spreads wide and widening

**Trading implication:** This is the hardest regime. Traditional diversification fails. Only commodities and gold provide real returns. Cash is being destroyed by inflation but is still safer than stocks or bonds. Position sizes must be small.

### 4. Deflationary Bust
**Characteristics:** Economic contraction, falling prices, potential debt crisis.
**Correlation matrix:**
- Bonds up sharply (flight to safety, rate cuts expected)
- Stocks down
- Commodities down (demand collapse)
- Gold mixed (deflationary force vs fear)
- Dollar up (debt deflation = dollar demand for repayment)
- VIX spiking
- Credit spreads blowing out

**Trading implication:** Long bonds, short equities, short commodities. Cash is king because deflation makes it worth more over time. This is the opposite of QE.

## Regime Detection

The agent must actively identify which regime is current. Use these signals:

**Growth indicators:** ISM above/below 50, GDP trend, employment trend.
**Inflation indicators:** CPI trend, PPI trend, breakeven inflation rates, commodity prices.
**Policy indicators:** Fed funds rate direction, balance sheet growing/shrinking, forward guidance.

Plot growth on one axis and inflation on the other:
- High growth + low inflation = Goldilocks
- Any growth + central bank flooding liquidity = QE regime
- Low growth + high inflation = Stagflation
- Negative growth + falling prices = Deflation

## Regime Transitions

The most profitable (and dangerous) periods are regime transitions. When the market is priced for one regime and shifts to another, every correlation flips and every position built for the old regime needs to be unwound.

**Detection:** Watch for sustained divergence from the expected correlation matrix. If you are in Goldilocks but stocks and bonds start falling together, the regime is shifting toward stagflation. If you are in QE but the dollar starts strengthening sharply, the regime may be transitioning to deflation.

**Action:** When you detect a regime shift, the first priority is to reduce positions built for the old regime. The second priority is to slowly build positions for the new regime. Do not flip aggressively — transitions can take months and false signals are common.

Always know which matrix you are operating in. The right trade in the wrong regime is a losing trade.
