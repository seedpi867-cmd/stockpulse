# News-to-Price Mapping: Translating Headlines into Trade Theses

## Core Principle

Not all news moves markets. The skill is not reading the headline — it is instantly mapping that headline to a structured impact assessment: which tickers, which direction, what magnitude, and over what timeframe. Practice this mapping BEFORE looking at the price. Then check if the market agreed with your mapping. That feedback loop is how you calibrate.

## The Mapping Framework

For every significant headline, run this mental template:

1. **Direct impact:** Which companies or sectors are directly named or affected?
2. **Direction:** Does this help or hurt them? By how much?
3. **Indirect impact:** Who benefits if the directly affected companies suffer? Who suffers if they benefit?
4. **Duration:** Is this a one-day event or a regime change? An earnings miss is one day. A new tariff regime is multi-quarter.
5. **Already priced in?** Was this expected? If the market was already positioned for this outcome, the move on the news will be muted or even reversed.

## Worked Examples

**"Fed raises rates 25bps"**
- Banks up: wider net interest margins (direct beneficiary)
- Tech down: higher discount rates reduce present value of future earnings
- Dollar up: higher rates attract foreign capital
- Gold down: higher rates increase opportunity cost of holding non-yielding assets
- Bonds down: inverse relationship between rates and bond prices
- REITs down: higher borrowing costs, competition from risk-free rates
- Duration: months — rate decisions persist until the next meeting

**"Tariff on Chinese semiconductors"**
- NVDA down: supply chain disruption, China revenue at risk
- Domestic chip makers (Intel, GlobalFoundries) up: competitive advantage in protected market
- ASML complicated: sells to everyone, but China orders may get cancelled while non-China demand increases
- Auto makers down: chip supply disruption hits production
- Defense up: domestic supply chain narrative strengthens
- Duration: quarters to years — tariffs are sticky policy

**"Oil spikes 15% on Middle East conflict"**
- Airlines crushed: fuel is 30% of operating costs
- Energy producers up: higher revenue per barrel
- Consumer discretionary down: higher gas prices reduce spending
- Walmart up: consumers trade down from premium to value retail
- Duration: depends on conflict — could be days or months

## The Calibration Process

After mapping, look at what actually happened. Score yourself:

- Did you get the direction right on the primary tickers?
- Did you identify second-order effects that actually played out?
- Did you correctly estimate the magnitude (overreacted vs under-reacted)?
- Was your timeframe estimate reasonable?

The gap between your prediction and reality is your learning signal. A large gap means your mental model of that relationship needs updating.

## Common Mistakes

**Treating all news equally.** A Fed policy change moves trillions of dollars. An analyst upgrade moves one stock for one day. Weight your attention accordingly.

**Ignoring the "priced in" factor.** If everyone expected 25bps and they got 25bps, the move was already made. The news is the deviation from expectation, not the event itself. A 25bps hike when 50bps was expected is actually dovish.

**Mapping only first-order effects.** Everyone sees oil up = energy stocks up. The agent that maps to the fourth-order effect (Walmart benefiting from consumer trade-down) is finding the uncrowded trade.

**Confusing short-term and long-term impact.** A tariff announcement causes an immediate selloff in affected stocks, but the long-term impact on domestic competitors might take quarters to materialize. Match your position sizing and holding period to the timeframe of the effect.

Build the map first. Check the price second. Calibrate constantly.
