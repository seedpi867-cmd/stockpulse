# Cross-Asset Signals: Reading One Market to Predict Another

## Core Principle

No asset moves in isolation. Every price movement in any market is connected to at least three others. Your job is not to react to a single ticker — it is to see the web of relationships and understand what the movement in one asset is telling you about the next.

## The Mental Model

Think of markets as a neural network. Bonds, currencies, commodities, and equities are nodes. When one node fires, the signal propagates through the network with predictable patterns and predictable delays. Your edge comes from tracing those signals faster than the market prices them in.

## Key Cross-Asset Relationships to Monitor

**Risk-off rotation sequence:** Bond yields drop → dollar weakens → gold rises → defensive equities outperform. When you see two of these three confirming, the third is coming. Do not wait for all three — by then the move is priced in.

**Semiconductor leadership:** Semis lead the NASDAQ by roughly two weeks. When SOX (semiconductor index) breaks out or breaks down, broad tech follows. This is because semis sit at the top of the technology supply chain — their orders reflect real demand 1-2 quarters forward.

**Credit leads equity:** Junk bond spreads (HYG, JNK, or the ICE BofA High Yield Index) widen before equity selloffs. Credit markets are smarter than equity markets because credit investors are asking "will this company survive?" while equity investors are asking "will this stock go up?" Survival questions get answered more accurately.

**Copper/gold ratio:** Copper is industrial demand. Gold is fear. The ratio between them predicts S&P 500 direction with surprising reliability. Rising copper/gold = economic expansion = stocks up. Falling copper/gold = contraction fears = stocks vulnerable.

**Dollar and emerging markets:** A strengthening dollar crushes emerging market equities and commodities priced in dollars. When DXY breaks out, get cautious on EM exposure immediately — do not wait for EM to actually sell off.

## The Process

Every time a significant price signal arrives — a breakout, a breakdown, a gap, a reversal — run it through this checklist:

1. What is this asset class telling me about risk appetite?
2. Which two other asset classes should confirm or deny this signal?
3. Are those confirming assets already moving, or is there a lag I can exploit?
4. If the other assets are NOT confirming, which one is wrong — the signal or the non-confirmation?

Do not form an opinion on any single asset until you have checked at least two cross-asset signals. A stock breakout means nothing if credit is deteriorating and the dollar is surging. A commodity selloff means nothing if industrial production data is actually improving.

## Common Traps

**Correlation is not constant.** Stocks and bonds are inversely correlated in normal markets but positively correlated during QE and during panics. Always check which regime you are in before applying cross-asset logic.

**Lagged signals feel wrong.** When copper is telling you the economy is weakening but stocks keep going up, it feels like copper is wrong. It is almost never wrong — it is just early. Trust the signal, manage the timing.

The agent that checks every signal against the web of cross-asset relationships will avoid most traps and find most opportunities before they become obvious.
