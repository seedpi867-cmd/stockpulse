# Wars and Market Impact

## The Historical Pattern

Markets hate uncertainty, and war creates the ultimate uncertainty. However, the trading axiom buy the rumor, sell the news applies in reverse to geopolitical conflict: sell the fear, buy the invasion. Markets tend to decline during the buildup to conflict and rally once hostilities actually begin, because the uncertainty resolves into a known (if terrible) reality.

## Case Studies

**Gulf War (1990-1991):** Iraq invaded Kuwait on August 2, 1990. The S&P 500 fell 17% from July to October 1990 during the buildup and uncertainty about US response. Oil spiked from 17 to 41 dollars per barrel. Once Operation Desert Storm launched on January 17, 1991, markets rallied sharply. The S&P 500 gained 25% from the October 1990 low through March 1991. Oil collapsed back to pre-invasion levels within weeks as fears of Middle East supply destruction proved overblown.

**September 11, 2001:** The attacks were a complete surprise with no buildup period. Markets closed for four trading days. On reopening, the S&P 500 fell 11.6% in the first week — the worst week since the Great Depression. However, markets recovered the entire loss within a month as the immediate shock faded and the Fed cut rates aggressively. Airlines and insurance stocks were devastated permanently, but defense contractors (Raytheon, Lockheed Martin, Northrop Grumman) began a multi-year rally.

**Iraq War (2003):** Markets declined from January through March 2003 as the invasion became increasingly certain. The S&P 500 bottomed on March 11, 2003 — nine days before the invasion on March 20. By the time bombs were falling on Baghdad, stocks were already rallying. The market gained 30% over the next 12 months.

**Russia-Ukraine (2022):** Russia began massing troops on the Ukrainian border in November 2021. Markets declined 8% from January through late February 2022 on escalation fears. The actual invasion on February 24, 2022 caused a one-day 2.1% drop in the S&P 500, but markets bottomed within days and rallied 8% over the next two weeks. European markets, particularly German and Polish indices, were hit harder. Energy stocks surged — the XLE energy ETF gained 64% in 2022 as European energy security fears drove oil and gas prices higher.

## Sector Playbook

During geopolitical escalation, the following sector patterns are remarkably consistent:

- **Defense/aerospace stocks rally:** Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, Northrop Grumman, General Dynamics all benefit from increased military spending. The ITA defense ETF typically outperforms within days of conflict escalation.
- **Energy surges:** Oil spikes on supply disruption fears. Energy stocks (XLE) are the primary hedge against geopolitical risk.
- **Safe havens bid:** Gold, US Treasuries, Swiss Franc, and Japanese Yen attract capital. Gold gained 25% in the 6 months following the 9/11 attacks.
- **Risk assets sell off initially:** Tech, consumer discretionary, and emerging markets see outflows during the fear phase.
- **Cybersecurity benefits:** Modern conflicts include cyber warfare. HACK and CIBR cybersecurity ETFs tend to rally on geopolitical tensions.

## Trading Framework

Position for the fear phase early (rotate to defense, energy, gold) when tensions rise but before conflict. When the first shot is fired or the invasion begins, start rotating back to risk assets — the worst of the uncertainty is typically behind you. The exception: conflicts that threaten global energy supply (Strait of Hormuz, Middle East oil infrastructure) or involve nuclear powers in direct confrontation. These scenarios do not follow the pattern because the potential downside is unbounded.
