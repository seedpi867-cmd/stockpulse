# Elections and Market Cycles

## The Presidential Cycle

The stock market exhibits a well-documented four-year pattern aligned with the US presidential election cycle, first identified by Yale Hirsch in 1968. The pattern persists because fiscal and monetary policy decisions are influenced by the political calendar.

**Year 1 (Post-Election):** Average S&P 500 return of 6.5%. The new president implements potentially unpopular policies (spending cuts, tax changes) while political capital is high. Markets often consolidate or correct as uncertainty about the new agenda plays out.

**Year 2 (Midterm):** The weakest year, averaging 4.0%. Midterm election uncertainty, combined with the lagged impact of Year 1 policy changes, creates the worst six-month stretch of the cycle. The S&P 500 has historically bottomed in the September-October window of midterm years.

**Year 3 (Pre-Election):** The strongest year, averaging 12.8%. The administration pumps the economy with fiscal stimulus and the Fed tends to accommodate. Since 1950, the S&P 500 has been positive in Year 3 of the cycle 88% of the time. Incumbents want a strong economy heading into the election.

**Year 4 (Election Year):** Average return of 7.5%. Returns are positive but volatile as election uncertainty creates two-way risk. Markets typically chop until the election outcome becomes clear, then rally regardless of winner.

## Election Night Patterns

Markets dislike uncertainty more than any particular outcome. The strongest post-election rallies occur when the result is decisive and clear. The weakest occur during contested or unclear outcomes:

- **2000 (Bush v. Gore):** 36 days of contested results. The S&P 500 fell 8% during the recount period, then bottomed with the Supreme Court decision in December.
- **2016 (Trump):** S&P 500 futures fell 5% overnight as the unexpected result was processed. By the next morning, futures had fully recovered and markets rallied 5% over the next month on tax cut expectations.
- **2020 (Biden):** Contested for several days, but markets rallied on election day and continued higher through year-end. The vaccine announcements in November 2020 overwhelmed election uncertainty.

## Policy Impact by Party

Historical data shows less difference between parties than most investors believe:

- **Average S&P 500 annual return under Democratic presidents:** 10.2% (1945-2024)
- **Average S&P 500 annual return under Republican presidents:** 6.9% (1945-2024)

This headline difference is misleading because it does not control for starting conditions. Republican presidents inherited overvalued markets more often (Nixon in 1969, Bush in 2001, Trump in 2020), while Democrats inherited undervalued markets (Obama in 2009, Biden in 2021). The president matters less than the economic cycle they inherit.

## What Actually Moves Markets

Specific policy changes matter more than party affiliation:

- **Tax cuts:** Immediate bullish catalyst. The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act drove a 7% S&P 500 rally in the month after passage. Corporate tax cuts directly boost EPS.
- **Regulatory changes:** Sector-specific. Healthcare stocks sell off under administrations proposing drug price controls. Energy stocks rally under deregulatory agendas. Financial stocks benefit from reduced capital requirements.
- **Trade policy:** Tariff announcements create immediate volatility. The US-China trade war (2018-2019) caused multiple 5-10% corrections on tariff escalation and rallies on de-escalation.
- **Fiscal spending:** Infrastructure bills benefit industrials and materials. Defense spending increases benefit aerospace and defense contractors.

## Trading Elections

Reduce position sizes 2-3 weeks before the election as implied volatility rises. Buy November expiration straddles (both calls and puts) to profit from the expected post-election volatility regardless of direction. After the result is clear, rotate into sectors that benefit from the winner policy agenda within the first week — this sector rotation typically persists for 3-6 months.
