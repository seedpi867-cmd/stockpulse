# Full Moon Trading: The Lunatic Fringe That Has Data

## The Study

Ilia Dichev and Troy Janes published "Lunar Cycle Effects in Stock Returns" in the Journal of Private Equity in 2003 (originally circulated as an SSRN working paper in 2001). The study examined US stock returns over 100 years and 24 other countries over 30 years. The finding: stock returns in the 15 days around new moons were roughly double the returns in the 15 days around full moons.

This is not a fringe blog post. It is a peer-reviewed academic paper using rigorous statistical methodology. The effect was robust across time periods, countries, and index construction methods. A follow-up study by Yuan, Zheng, and Zhu (2006), "Are Investors Moonstruck? Lunar Phases and Stock Returns" in the Journal of Empirical Finance, confirmed the finding using a different methodology and dataset.

## The Numbers

In the Dichev-Janes study, the annualised return during the 15-day new moon window was significantly higher than during the full moon window. The difference was economically meaningful — several percentage points annualised. In Yuan et al., returns around new moons were 3-5% higher annually than around full moons across the 48 countries studied.

Importantly, the studies found no lunar cycle effect on trading volume or volatility — only on returns. This rules out some explanations (like reduced liquidity during certain moon phases) and points toward a behavioural mechanism.

## The Proposed Mechanism

The hypothesis is psychological. Research in psychology and biology has documented that lunar cycles affect human mood, sleep quality, and behaviour (though this is debated). Full moons are associated with disrupted sleep and heightened anxiety in some studies. If traders are slightly more anxious or risk-averse during full moon periods, they might be marginally less willing to buy risky assets, creating a small but persistent drag on returns.

The effect is too small for transaction costs to make it profitably tradeable in isolation for most investors. After commissions, slippage, and taxes, the 3-5% annual return difference is largely consumed. But the finding is statistically real and highlights something important about markets: human psychology — including subconscious, biologically-driven mood shifts — leaks into asset prices in measurable ways.

## Why This Matters for the Agent

This file is not included because the agent should trade on moon phases. It is included for three reasons.

First, it demonstrates that non-obvious, even absurd-sounding factors can have statistically significant effects on markets. The agent should maintain epistemic humility about what drives prices.

Second, it illustrates the difference between statistical significance and economic significance. The lunar effect is statistically real but not economically exploitable after costs. Many "edges" in finance have this property — real in data, useless in practice.

Third, it serves as a calibration point for evaluating other unconventional signals. If a peer-reviewed study in a respectable journal can document a moon phase effect, then other unconventional factors (weather, sports outcomes, holidays) should not be dismissed out of hand without examination.

## The Broader Lesson

Markets are made of humans, and humans are biological organisms subject to circadian rhythms, weather effects, hormonal cycles, and subconscious influences. The efficient market hypothesis assumes rational actors. Behavioural finance shows that actors are predictably irrational. The lunar cycle effect is an extreme example of predictable irrationality operating at a level below conscious awareness.

## What the Agent Should Know

Log the data as a known anomaly. Do not trade on it directly. Use it as a lens for evaluating other unconventional signals and as a reminder that the boundary between signal and noise in financial markets is stranger than most practitioners assume.
