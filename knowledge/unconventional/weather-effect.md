# Weather Effect: Sunshine and Stock Returns

## The Study

David Hirshleifer and Tyler Shumway published "Good Day Sunshine: Stock Returns and the Weather" in the Journal of Finance in 2003. They examined the relationship between morning sunshine in the city of the stock exchange and daily stock returns across 26 countries from 1982 to 1997. The result: sunshine was positively and significantly correlated with stock returns. Cloud cover in the city where the exchange is located predicted lower returns that day.

This was published in the Journal of Finance — one of the three most prestigious academic finance journals in the world. The editors and reviewers accepted it because the methodology was sound and the statistical significance was robust.

## The Numbers

After controlling for other known effects (day of week, month, holidays, etc.), the sunshine effect was statistically significant in 18 of 26 markets studied. The effect was strongest in markets where the exchange was located in a city with high weather variability. The economic magnitude was small — a few basis points per day — but consistent and statistically reliable.

A hypothetical strategy of going long on perfectly sunny days and short on completely overcast days generated annualised excess returns, but after transaction costs the profits were marginal. Like the lunar effect, this is a real signal that is difficult to exploit profitably.

## The Mechanism

The mechanism is well-understood from psychology: sunshine improves mood. Improved mood makes people more optimistic, more willing to take risks, and more likely to evaluate ambiguous information positively. Since many trading decisions involve ambiguous information (is this earnings report good or bad? is this news bullish or bearish?), a positive mood bias — even a slight one — tips the marginal decisions toward buying.

This is not about individual traders consciously thinking "it is sunny so I will buy stocks." It operates below awareness. Aggregated across thousands of traders in a financial centre, the effect becomes statistically detectable.

## Extensions and Related Research

Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD) and stock returns: Kamstra, Kramer, and Levi (2003) found that stock returns are lower during fall and winter months in both hemispheres, consistent with SAD-induced risk aversion during shorter days. The effect reverses as days lengthen.

Temperature and aggression: Cao and Wei (2005) found that extreme temperatures — both hot and cold — are associated with lower stock returns, possibly because discomfort reduces risk appetite.

Geomagnetic storms: Krivelyova and Robotti (2003) found that geomagnetic storms (caused by solar activity) are associated with lower stock returns for several days following the storm. Geomagnetic activity is known to affect melatonin production and mood.

Taken together, these studies paint a consistent picture: environmental factors that affect human mood have small but measurable effects on aggregate market behaviour.

## Practical Implications

The weather effect is not directly tradeable due to its small magnitude. Its value is conceptual. It demonstrates that market participants are biological organisms whose decision-making is influenced by environmental factors they are not consciously aware of. This has implications for understanding why markets sometimes behave irrationally for no apparent fundamental reason — the reason might be biological, not informational.

For the agent, weather data for major financial centres (New York, London, Tokyo, Shanghai) is freely available and could serve as a minor input into sentiment models. On its own it is worthless, but as one factor in a multi-factor sentiment model it adds a small amount of orthogonal information.

## What the Agent Should Know

Weather effects are real, documented in top-tier journals, and too small to trade on their own. Their primary value is in understanding market microstructure — why returns vary in ways that fundamental analysis cannot explain — and as minor inputs into broader sentiment frameworks.
