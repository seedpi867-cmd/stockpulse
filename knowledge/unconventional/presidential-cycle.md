# Presidential Cycle: The Political Machine That Moves Markets

## The Pattern

Since 1950, the third year of a US presidential term (the pre-election year) has been the strongest for stock returns, averaging roughly 16% for the S&P 500. The first year (post-inauguration) averages roughly 6%. The second year averages about 4%. The fourth year (election year) averages about 7%.

This is not a marginal effect. Year 3 outperforms Year 2 by a factor of four on average. The pattern has persisted across Republican and Democratic administrations, across bull and bear markets, and across different economic regimes.

## Why It Works

The mechanism is political incentive, not market randomness. The sitting president (or their party) wants to win re-election. The policy playbook is consistent regardless of party:

Year 1 (post-inauguration): the new administration pushes through unpopular but necessary policies — tax changes, regulatory shifts, spending adjustments. The electoral mandate is fresh and political capital is high. Markets often struggle as uncertainty about the new policy direction weighs on confidence.

Year 2 (midterm year): the administration continues policy implementation. Midterm elections create additional uncertainty. This is historically the weakest year for stocks. Market corrections disproportionately occur in Year 2 — the 1962, 1966, 1974, 1978, 1990, 2002, 2010, and 2022 selloffs all fell in midterm years.

Year 3 (pre-election year): the administration shifts to economic stimulus mode. Tax cuts, spending increases, accommodative regulatory stances, and pressure on the Federal Reserve to keep conditions loose. The goal is to create a strong economy heading into the election. Markets respond to the policy tailwind.

Year 4 (election year): the economy is typically humming from Year 3 stimulus. Returns are positive but moderate as uncertainty about the election outcome caps enthusiasm. Markets generally rally after the election, regardless of which party wins, because uncertainty resolves.

## The Midterm Low

A refinement of the presidential cycle is the "midterm low" — the market tends to find its cycle bottom in Year 2, typically in September or October (the historically weakest period within the historically weakest year). Since 1950, buying the S&P 500 at the midterm year low and holding for 12 months has produced positive returns in every single instance.

This is an extraordinary stat. No other entry timing rule in equity markets has a 100% hit rate over 70+ years.

## The Fed Connection

The presidential cycle is partially explained by Federal Reserve policy. While the Fed is nominally independent, research shows that monetary policy tends to be easier in pre-election years. Whether this is direct political pressure or the Fed responding to the same economic conditions that the administration is engineering, the effect is the same: lower rates and easier financial conditions in Year 3 support higher stock prices.

## Limitations

The cycle is a tendency, not a guarantee. Year 3 returns range from significantly negative (2015, depending on measurement) to extremely positive (2019: +29%). The average is pulled up by the strong years, but individual years can deviate substantially.

The pattern may also be degrading as markets become more efficient and more globally integrated. Domestic political cycles matter less when global monetary policy, geopolitics, and technological disruption are driving market returns.

## What the Agent Should Know

Track the presidential cycle as a background factor in market timing. Weight bullish signals more heavily in Year 3 and bearish signals more heavily in Year 2. The midterm low is particularly actionable — be prepared to deploy capital aggressively in September-October of midterm years. The cycle is one input among many, but it has a longer and more consistent track record than most technical or seasonal indicators.
