# Options Fundamentals for Stock Traders

## What Options Are

An option is a contract giving the buyer the right (but not the obligation) to buy or sell an underlying asset at a specified price (strike price) before a specified date (expiration). A call option gives the right to buy; a put option gives the right to sell. The buyer pays a premium for this right. The seller (writer) collects the premium and takes on the obligation.

## The Greeks

Options prices are driven by five key variables, collectively called the Greeks:

**Delta:** How much the option price changes per one-dollar move in the underlying stock. A call with delta 0.50 gains 0.50 for every 1.00 the stock rises. At-the-money options have delta near 0.50; deep in-the-money options approach 1.00 (behaving like stock); far out-of-the-money options have delta near 0. Delta also approximates the probability the option expires in the money.

**Gamma:** The rate of change of delta. Gamma is highest for at-the-money options near expiration. High gamma means delta changes rapidly with small stock moves, creating explosive profit or loss potential. The last week before expiration, at-the-money options have extreme gamma — a 2% stock move can double or zero the option.

**Theta:** Time decay — the amount the option loses in value each day, all else equal. Options are wasting assets. A 30-day at-the-money option loses roughly 3-4% of its value per day in the final week. This is why option buyers need the stock to move quickly and directionally, while option sellers profit from the passage of time.

**Vega:** Sensitivity to changes in implied volatility. A 1-point increase in IV increases the option price by its vega value. Before earnings, IV rises as uncertainty increases, inflating option premiums. After earnings, IV collapses (the IV crush), and option prices drop even if the stock moves in the expected direction. Many traders lose money buying options before earnings because the IV crush destroys their premium.

**Rho:** Sensitivity to interest rate changes. Minor for short-term options, more relevant for LEAPS (options with 1-2 years until expiration). Higher interest rates increase call values and decrease put values.

## Core Strategies for Stock Traders

**Protective puts:** Own stock and buy a put below the current price as insurance. The put profits if the stock falls, offsetting losses. Cost: the put premium, which reduces returns if the stock rises or stays flat. Equivalent to paying for portfolio insurance. Best purchased when VIX is low and puts are cheap.

**Covered calls:** Own stock and sell a call above the current price. You collect premium (income) but cap your upside at the strike price. If the stock stays flat or rises modestly, you keep the premium. If it surges past the strike, your stock gets called away. This strategy works best in sideways to moderately bullish markets. It reduces portfolio volatility and generates 2-4% additional annual income.

**Vertical spreads:** Buy one option and sell another at a different strike, same expiration. A bull call spread (buy the 100 call, sell the 105 call) limits both risk and reward. Maximum loss is the net premium paid. Maximum gain is the difference between strikes minus premium. Spreads are more capital-efficient than buying options outright and reduce the impact of time decay and IV changes.

## Key Concepts

**Implied volatility (IV) rank:** IV rank measures where current IV sits relative to its 52-week range. An IV rank of 80% means current IV is near its annual high — options are expensive. An IV rank of 20% means options are cheap. Buy options when IV rank is low (cheap insurance), sell options when IV rank is high (expensive premium to collect).

**Maximum pain (max pain):** The strike price at which the total value of all outstanding options (both puts and calls) would result in the maximum loss for option holders. There is evidence that stocks gravitate toward max pain on expiration day as dealers hedge their positions, creating a magnetic effect on price.

**Options expiration (OpEx):** Monthly options expire the third Friday of each month. The week of expiration often sees increased volatility as gamma effects intensify and dealers adjust hedges. The Monday after OpEx frequently sees a directional move as the hedging pressure lifts.
