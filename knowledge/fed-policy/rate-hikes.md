# Fed Rate Hike Cycles and Market Impact

## How Rate Hikes Work

The Federal Reserve raises the federal funds rate (the overnight interbank lending rate) to cool an overheating economy and fight inflation. Higher rates increase the cost of borrowing for consumers and businesses, slow economic activity, and typically pressure asset valuations — particularly for growth stocks whose value depends on future cash flows discounted at higher rates.

## Historical Rate Hike Cycles

**2022-2023 (Current Relevance):** The most aggressive hiking cycle in 40 years. The Fed raised rates from near-zero (0.00-0.25%) to 5.25-5.50% between March 2022 and July 2023 — a total of 525 basis points across 11 hikes. This included four consecutive 75bp hikes (June-November 2022), the first 75bp moves since 1994. The S&P 500 fell 25% during 2022, and the NASDAQ fell 33%. The 60/40 portfolio had its worst year since 1937.

**2015-2018:** A gradual cycle. Nine hikes of 25bp each, from 0.00-0.25% to 2.25-2.50% over three years. Markets handled this well because the pace was slow and well-telegraphed. The S&P 500 gained 48% during this hiking cycle. However, the final hike in December 2018 triggered a 19.8% selloff as markets priced in over-tightening.

**2004-2006:** Seventeen consecutive 25bp hikes, taking rates from 1.00% to 5.25%. Markets rose through most of the cycle — the S&P 500 gained 12% — as economic growth outpaced the drag from higher rates. But the endpoint (5.25%) ultimately popped the housing bubble and led to the 2008 crisis.

**1999-2000:** Six hikes from 4.75% to 6.50%. The final hike in May 2000 came two months after the NASDAQ peak, accelerating the dot-com crash.

## Market Behavior During Hike Cycles

**Early hikes are bullish.** The first 1-3 rate hikes typically accompany a strong economy. Stocks often rally because the economic strength that prompted hikes outweighs the marginal cost of higher rates. The S&P 500 has averaged positive returns in the 6 months following the first rate hike of a cycle.

**Mid-cycle is volatile.** As rates rise above neutral (estimated at 2.5-3.0%), the economy begins to feel the drag. Growth slows, corporate margins compress, and markets become choppy. Sector rotation from growth to value accelerates.

**Late hikes are dangerous.** The final 1-2 hikes of a cycle often represent over-tightening. The yield curve inverts (2-year Treasury yield exceeds 10-year), which has preceded every recession since 1970 with only one false signal (1966). When the 2/10 spread inverted in July 2022, a recession became the base case within 12-24 months.

## What To Watch

- **Fed dot plot:** Quarterly projections of where each FOMC member expects rates. The median dot drives market expectations.
- **CME FedWatch Tool:** Real-time market-implied probabilities for the next meeting decision. When FedWatch shows >80% probability of a hike, it is priced in; the trade is on the unexpected outcome.
- **2/10 yield curve:** The spread between 2-year and 10-year Treasury yields. Inversion signals the market expects rate cuts (economic weakness) ahead.
- **Real rates:** The fed funds rate minus inflation. Negative real rates are stimulative even if nominal rates are rising. Real rates turned positive in early 2023, marking the point where policy became genuinely restrictive.
