# Quantitative Easing (QE) and Quantitative Tightening (QT)

## What QE Is

Quantitative easing is the Fed purchasing government bonds and mortgage-backed securities (MBS) from the open market, injecting cash into the financial system. When the Fed buys a Treasury bond from a bank, the bank now holds cash instead of a bond — that cash can be lent out or invested, increasing liquidity throughout the economy. QE is used when interest rates are already at or near zero and the Fed needs additional stimulus tools.

## QE Episodes

**QE1 (November 2008 - March 2010):** 1.75 trillion dollars in purchases (1.25T in MBS, 300B in Treasuries, 200B in agency debt). Launched during the worst of the financial crisis. The S&P 500 bottomed in March 2009 and rallied 80% by the end of QE1. Credit spreads narrowed from crisis levels, and the corporate bond market reopened.

**QE2 (November 2010 - June 2011):** 600 billion dollars in Treasury purchases. More controversial — critics warned of hyperinflation (which never materialized). The S&P 500 gained 24% during QE2. Commodities surged — oil went from 83 to 115 dollars, gold from 1,350 to 1,500 dollars.

**Operation Twist (September 2011 - December 2012):** Not technically QE — the Fed sold short-term Treasuries and bought long-term Treasuries to flatten the yield curve. No net expansion of the balance sheet. Modest market impact.

**QE3 (September 2012 - October 2014):** Open-ended — 85 billion dollars per month in purchases (40B MBS, 45B Treasuries) with no preset end date. Eventually tapered starting in January 2014. The S&P 500 gained 43% during QE3. This program cemented the rule of not fighting the Fed as the dominant market mantra.

**COVID QE (March 2020 - March 2022):** The most aggressive QE ever. The Fed purchased 120 billion per month (80B Treasuries, 40B MBS), expanding its balance sheet from 4.2 trillion to 8.9 trillion dollars. Crucially, the Fed also bought corporate bond ETFs for the first time — both investment grade and high yield — effectively backstopping the entire credit market. This drove the fastest bear market recovery in history.

## The Fed Put

QE created the market concept of the Fed put — the belief that the Fed will step in to support markets during significant declines. This is not imaginary: every major market stress event since 2008 (2011 European crisis, 2015 China fears, 2018 Q4 selloff, 2020 COVID) was met with Fed easing. The practical effect is that drawdowns are bought more aggressively because investors trust the backstop.

## Quantitative Tightening (QT)

QT is the reverse — the Fed allows bonds on its balance sheet to mature without reinvesting the proceeds, draining liquidity. QT began in June 2022 at 95 billion per month (60B Treasuries, 35B MBS). This reduces bank reserves and tightens financial conditions without explicitly raising rates. The first QT attempt (2017-2019) ended abruptly in September 2019 when the repo market spiked, exposing how dependent the system had become on excess reserves.

## Trading Implications

QE is rocket fuel for risk assets. When the Fed is expanding its balance sheet, the path of least resistance is up. Growth stocks, high-yield bonds, and speculative assets benefit most because QE compresses risk premiums. QT creates a headwind — not necessarily a crash, but reduced liquidity means lower multiples and less tolerance for disappointing earnings. Track the Fed balance sheet weekly (published every Thursday at federalreserve.gov) as a meta-indicator for market conditions.
