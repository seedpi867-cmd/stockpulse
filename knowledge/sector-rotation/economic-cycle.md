# Sector Rotation Through the Economic Cycle

## The Business Cycle Framework

Sam Stovall of S&P Global codified the relationship between economic phases and sector leadership. The economy moves through four phases — early expansion, mid expansion, late expansion, and recession — and different sectors lead in each phase. Understanding where you are in the cycle determines which sectors to overweight and underweight.

## Phase 1: Early Expansion (Recovery)

Economic conditions: GDP growth accelerating from trough, unemployment still high but peaking, consumer confidence rising from lows, Fed policy still accommodative, yield curve steep.

Leading sectors:
- **Financials:** Banks profit from a steep yield curve (borrow short, lend long). Loan losses peak and begin declining. Bank stocks typically lead the market off recession lows — the Financial Select Sector SPDR (XLF) gained 170% from March 2009 to April 2010.
- **Consumer Discretionary:** Pent-up demand releases as consumers begin spending again. Homebuilders, auto manufacturers, and retail stocks rally aggressively. Amazon, Home Depot, and Nike are classic early-cycle outperformers.
- **Technology:** Capital spending resumes as corporate confidence returns. Semiconductor stocks (SOX index) are particularly cyclical and tend to lead, often gaining 50-100% in the first 12 months of recovery.
- **Industrials:** Manufacturing recovery, restocking inventory, and infrastructure spending drive outperformance.

## Phase 2: Mid Expansion (Growth)

Economic conditions: GDP growth at or above trend, unemployment falling steadily, corporate earnings growing 10-15% annually, Fed beginning to normalize policy, yield curve flattening.

Leading sectors:
- **Technology:** Continues to lead as enterprise IT spending accelerates. Software and cloud companies show the strongest earnings growth in this phase.
- **Communication Services:** Ad spending grows with the economy. Media and entertainment benefit from consumer spending growth.
- **Industrials:** Order books fill. Capital expenditure cycles peak. Transportation stocks (rails, trucking) benefit from rising freight volumes.

## Phase 3: Late Expansion (Overheating)

Economic conditions: GDP growth decelerating, inflation rising, unemployment at cycle lows, wage pressure building, Fed tightening aggressively, yield curve flat or inverted.

Leading sectors:
- **Energy:** Commodity prices peak late in the cycle as demand has been building for years and supply cannot respond quickly. The XLE energy sector gained 65% in 2022 during late-cycle inflation.
- **Materials:** Mining, chemicals, and commodity producers benefit from high input prices.
- **Healthcare:** Defensive growth — consistent earnings regardless of cycle. Pharmaceuticals and managed care outperform as investors rotate from cyclicals to quality.

## Phase 4: Recession (Contraction)

Economic conditions: GDP declining, unemployment rising, corporate earnings falling, Fed cutting rates, yield curve steepening from inversion.

Leading sectors:
- **Consumer Staples:** Procter & Gamble, Coca-Cola, Walmart — people still buy toothpaste and groceries. The XLP consumer staples ETF outperformed the S&P 500 by 23% during the 2008 recession.
- **Utilities:** Bond-like yield stocks attract capital as rates fall. Regulated utilities have predictable cash flows regardless of economic conditions.
- **Healthcare:** Continues to outperform. Healthcare spending is largely non-discretionary.
- **Real Estate (REITs):** Benefit from falling rates. REITs typically bottom before the broader market as the rate-sensitive discount rate drops.

## Practical Implementation

Use the ISM Manufacturing PMI as the primary cycle indicator. Readings above 50 indicate expansion, below 50 contraction. The direction of change matters more than the level: PMI rising from 47 to 52 (accelerating) favors early-cycle sectors; PMI falling from 58 to 53 (decelerating but still expanding) favors late-cycle sectors.

Rotate sector ETF allocations quarterly based on the cycle phase. Overweight 2-3 leading sectors with 40-50% of equity allocation, market-weight 3-4 sectors, and underweight 2-3 lagging sectors. This systematic approach has added 2-4% annually to returns versus static equal-weight sector allocation based on backtests from 1990 to 2023.
