# Liquidity Cycles — The Tide That Lifts and Sinks All Boats

## The Uncomfortable Truth

Markets run on liquidity, not fundamentals. When central banks print money and inject liquidity into the financial system, asset prices rise — almost everything goes up, including assets with no fundamental value. When central banks tighten and drain liquidity, asset prices eventually fall — even high-quality assets with strong fundamentals.

This is uncomfortable because it means the careful fundamental analysis of earnings, cash flows, and competitive moats is secondary to a single question: is global liquidity expanding or contracting?

## The Evidence

The correlation between global central bank balance sheet expansion and equity returns is striking:

- **2009-2014 (QE1, QE2, QE3):** The Fed's balance sheet expanded from $900 billion to $4.5 trillion. S&P 500 rose approximately 180%.
- **2017-2019 (QT1):** The Fed began reducing its balance sheet. S&P 500 had its worst December since 1931 (December 2018) as liquidity drained.
- **2020-2021 (COVID QE):** Global central banks injected roughly $10 trillion. Everything rallied — stocks, bonds, crypto, housing, even meme stocks and NFTs. Quality and garbage rose together because the liquidity tide lifted all boats.
- **2022-2023 (QT2):** The Fed began aggressive balance sheet reduction and rate hikes. S&P 500 fell 25%. Bitcoin fell 75%. Speculative assets collapsed. Even quality growth stocks fell 50-70%.
- **2023-2024:** Despite continued QT, the ON RRP drain released $2+ trillion (see reverse-repo.md). Net liquidity stabilized, and markets rallied.

## Measuring Global Liquidity

The simplified formula: **Net Liquidity = Central Bank Balance Sheet - Treasury General Account (TGA) - Reverse Repo (RRP)**

This captures the three main plumbing components:
- **Balance sheet growth** = liquidity injection
- **TGA growth** = government draining liquidity (building cash reserves)
- **RRP growth** = money parked at the Fed, not in markets

For global liquidity, add the balance sheets of the Fed, ECB, BOJ, PBOC, and BOE. These five central banks control the vast majority of global liquidity. When all five are expanding simultaneously (as in 2020), the liquidity tsunami is overwhelming. When they diverge (Fed tightening while BOJ eases, for example), effects partially offset.

## The Lag

Liquidity changes lead asset prices by approximately 2-12 weeks, depending on the magnitude of the change and existing market conditions. Small liquidity changes take longer to manifest in prices. Large changes (like emergency QE) manifest almost immediately because market participants anticipate the effect.

## How to Track

- **Fed balance sheet:** Published weekly on the Fed's H.4.1 report (federalreserve.gov). Updates every Thursday.
- **TGA balance:** Published daily by the Treasury Department (fiscal.treasury.gov/reports-statements/dts).
- **RRP balance:** Published daily by the NY Fed (see reverse-repo.md).
- **Global aggregate:** CrossBorderCapital publishes a Global Liquidity Index. Michael Howell's research on liquidity cycles is the academic foundation for this framework.

## The Strategy Implications

| Liquidity Environment | Strategy |
|----------------------|----------|
| Expanding rapidly | Risk on. Long equities, especially beta and speculative names. This is when momentum strategies work best. |
| Expanding slowly | Mild risk on. Favor quality growth over speculative. Momentum works but with smaller returns. |
| Flat/stable | Neutral. Stock selection and sector rotation matter more than direction. |
| Contracting slowly | Risk off. Favor defensive sectors, high-quality bonds, cash. Momentum strategies start failing. |
| Contracting rapidly | Defensive. Cash is king. Short positions or long volatility. Do not fight the liquidity drain. |

## The Agent's Role

Track net liquidity weekly. Calculate the 4-week rate of change. When the rate of change turns from positive to negative (or vice versa), flag it as a regime change signal. Combine with VIX regime and sector leadership data for a complete macro picture. Liquidity direction should be the first filter before any trade decision — swimming against the liquidity tide is the most common way sophisticated traders lose money.
