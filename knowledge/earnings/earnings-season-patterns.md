# Earnings Season Patterns and Calendar Effects

## Earnings Season Structure

Earnings season occurs four times per year, roughly 2-4 weeks after each quarter ends. The major banks (JPMorgan, Citigroup, Wells Fargo, Goldman Sachs) traditionally kick off each season during the second week of the month following quarter end. The season then builds in intensity over the next 3-4 weeks as hundreds of companies report.

Q1 reports: mid-April through late May
Q2 reports: mid-July through late August
Q3 reports: mid-October through late November
Q4 reports: mid-January through late February

## Bank Earnings Set the Tone

The big bank reports in the first week are disproportionately important because they set the narrative for the entire season. If JPMorgan beats and guides higher, citing strong consumer spending and credit quality, it creates a bullish backdrop. If banks report rising loan losses and cautious guidance, the entire season trades with a bearish undertone. The KBE and XLF bank ETFs on the first day of bank earnings often predict the direction of the broader market for the next 4-6 weeks.

## The Earnings Recession Indicator

Two consecutive quarters of year-over-year S&P 500 EPS decline constitutes an earnings recession. This has preceded or coincided with an economic recession approximately 70% of the time. Track the FactSet blended earnings growth rate (which combines reported results with estimates for companies that have not yet reported) weekly during the season. When the blended rate turns negative, risk management tightens significantly.

S&P 500 earnings turned negative in Q1-Q3 2020 (COVID), Q3-Q4 2022 to Q1-Q2 2023 (rate hikes), and preceded the 2008 recession by two quarters. Monitoring the trajectory of earnings growth is more valuable than any single economic indicator.

## Conference Call Analysis

The earnings call is often more important than the numbers themselves. Key phrases to watch:

- Macro softness and pipeline delays signal future revenue misses. These qualitative warnings from management are more predictive than the quantitative estimates.
- Raising full-year guidance is the strongest bullish signal, especially when accompanied by specific drivers rather than vague optimism.
- Prudent cost management is often a euphemism for demand weakness — the company is cutting costs because revenue growth is slowing.
- Management tone matters — hesitation, excessive hedging language, or unusual emphasis on non-GAAP metrics (excluding restructuring, stock-based compensation) can signal discomfort with underlying trends.

## Calendar Anomalies

**Pre-announcement effect:** The 5 trading days before a company reports earnings show abnormal positive returns on average (0.5-1.0%), driven by anticipatory buying and short covering. This effect is weaker in bear markets.

**Post-earnings announcement drift (PEAD):** As discussed in the guidance file, stocks drift in the direction of the earnings surprise for 60-90 days. This is the most exploitable earnings anomaly.

**Earnings quality decay:** Companies that consistently beat by exactly 1-2 cents, never miss, and show smooth quarter-over-quarter growth may be managing earnings through accruals and reserves. When such companies finally miss, the reaction is catastrophic because the miss reveals that prior beats were manufactured. General Electric under Jeff Immelt was a textbook example — years of managed earnings followed by massive writedowns starting in 2017.

## Sector Reporting Patterns

Technology companies report early-to-mid season and set the growth narrative. Consumer companies report mid-season and reveal spending trends. Retail reports last (due to fiscal calendars) and provide the final read on consumer health. Trading the sector ETFs directionally based on the early reporters in each sector and extrapolating to the later reporters is a viable earnings season strategy.
