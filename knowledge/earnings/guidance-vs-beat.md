# Earnings: Guidance vs. Beat

## The Earnings Game

Quarterly earnings reports are the most important recurring catalyst for individual stock prices. However, the market reaction is not about the absolute numbers — it is about the numbers relative to expectations and, crucially, about forward guidance. A company can beat earnings estimates and still crash 20% if guidance disappoints. Understanding this dynamic is essential for trading around earnings.

## The Beat/Miss Framework

Wall Street analysts publish earnings per share (EPS) and revenue estimates before each quarterly report. The consensus estimate (average of all analyst estimates) becomes the benchmark. Companies have systematically managed expectations so that roughly 75% of S&P 500 companies beat EPS estimates in any given quarter. This means beating consensus is the baseline expectation — it is priced in.

What matters is the magnitude of the beat and the quality of the guidance:

- **Beat and raise:** Company beats EPS estimates and raises forward guidance. This is the strongest bullish catalyst. Stocks that beat and raise outperform the market by an average of 3-5% in the month following earnings.
- **Beat and maintain:** Company beats but keeps guidance unchanged. Modest positive reaction. The beat was expected; no new information in guidance.
- **Beat and lower:** Company beats the current quarter but lowers forward guidance. This is typically bearish despite the headline beat. The market is forward-looking — lowered guidance reprices future earnings downward. Meta fell 26% after hours in February 2022 despite beating Q4 estimates, because it guided for weak Q1 revenue.
- **Miss and lower:** The worst outcome. EPS miss combined with lowered guidance triggers aggressive selling, often 10-20% gap downs. These are typically not buyable immediately — the first analyst downgrades arrive in the following days, creating additional selling pressure.

## The Whisper Number

Beyond the published consensus, there is an unofficial whisper number — what buy-side traders and institutional investors actually expect. The whisper is typically 2-5% above consensus because the analyst game is to set a low bar. When a company beats consensus but misses the whisper, the stock sells off despite the headline beat. ## Pre-Earnings Positioning

**Earnings drift:** Stocks tend to drift in the direction of their eventual earnings surprise in the 30 days before the report. Companies that will beat tend to drift higher (as insiders, suppliers, and channel-checking analysts accumulate), while those that will miss drift lower. This pre-earnings drift is a documented anomaly (Bernard and Thomas, 1989) that persists because acting on it requires taking risk before the known catalyst.

**Implied move:** Options markets price the expected post-earnings move via the implied volatility of at-the-money straddles expiring after the report. If the straddle costs 8% of the stock price, the market expects an 8% move in either direction. Historically, actual moves exceed the implied move roughly 40% of the time. Selling the straddle (selling the expected move) is profitable on average but carries significant tail risk when actual moves are 2-3x the implied.

## Post-Earnings Drift

One of the most robust anomalies in finance: stocks that beat estimates continue to outperform for 60-90 days after the report, and stocks that miss continue to underperform. This happens because the market underreacts to earnings information on the day. Buying stocks that gap up 5%+ on strong earnings and guidance, with institutional-quality volume, and holding for 3-6 weeks captures this drift. The effect is strongest for small and mid-cap stocks where information diffuses more slowly.

## Practical Rules

Never hold a full position through earnings unless you have a strong conviction on the outcome. Reduce to half-size before the report and add back after if the reaction confirms your thesis. Never average down on an earnings gap-down — the first reaction is usually right for at least 2-3 weeks. Watch the first 30 minutes of trading after the open: if a stock gaps down on earnings but recovers its losses by 10:00 AM on heavy volume, the selling was overdone and a reversal trade is viable.
