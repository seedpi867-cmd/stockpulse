# Narrative Momentum — Stories Move Markets More Than Numbers

## The Reality

Markets trade on narratives, not spreadsheets. The most powerful price moves in history have been driven by stories: "The internet will change everything" (1999), "Housing never goes down" (2006), "AI will transform every industry" (2023-2024), "Meme stocks to the moon" (2021).

A narrative is a shared belief that simplifies a complex reality into a tradeable thesis. Numbers confirm or deny the narrative, but the narrative determines which numbers get attention and how they are interpreted.

NVIDIA went from $150 to $900+ between 2023 and 2024. The earnings growth was real, but the magnitude of the price move was driven by the AI narrative — the story that AI would be as transformative as the internet, that NVIDIA's GPUs were the essential infrastructure, and that demand would be insatiable for years. The narrative drew in capital that exceeded what fundamentals alone justified, creating a momentum loop.

## How Narratives Drive Price

1. **Initial adopters.** Smart money identifies a narrative early and builds positions. Prices move modestly.
2. **Media amplification.** Financial media picks up the story. Articles proliferate. Analyst reports multiply. The narrative gains vocabulary — "picks and shovels" for AI, "network effects" for social media, "recurring revenue" for SaaS.
3. **Institutional adoption.** Fund managers who need to justify their positions to clients adopt the narrative. "We are positioned for the AI revolution" is easier to sell than "we did regression analysis on NVIDIA's datacenter revenue." Institutional buying accelerates price movement.
4. **Retail participation.** The narrative reaches retail investors through social media, YouTube, and conversations. Retail inflows provide the final wave of buying.
5. **Self-reinforcing confirmation.** Rising prices confirm the narrative. "NVIDIA is up 200%, AI must be real." The narrative creates the returns that validate the narrative. This is reflexivity (see reflexivity.md).

## Trading with Narratives

The edge is not in identifying the narrative — by the time you can articulate it clearly, the early money has been made. The edge is in two things:

**Narrative persistence:** once a narrative takes hold, it persists longer than fundamentalists expect. "AI is overhyped" was a reasonable thesis in early 2023, but trading against the narrative was ruinously expensive. Narratives can sustain price moves for 12-24 months or longer, even when valuations appear stretched.

**Narrative transitions:** the real opportunity is identifying when one narrative is dying and another is being born. The death of "growth at any price" in 2022 and the birth of "AI infrastructure" in 2023 was a narrative transition. The money that moved from one story to the next was the smart money.

## Identifying the Dominant Narrative

For the agent:
- Track the most-used phrases in financial media (Bloomberg, CNBC, Financial Times) over rolling 30-day windows. The dominant narrative reveals itself through language frequency.
- Monitor sector ETF flows alongside media coverage. When capital follows the story, the narrative has institutional backing.
- Track Google Trends for financial keywords. "AI stocks," "energy stocks," "recession" — the search volume tells you what the public narrative is and how strong it is.
- Listen for narrative vocabulary entering earnings calls. When CEOs start using the dominant narrative's language ("AI-enabled," "generative AI pipeline"), they are trying to attach their company to the narrative for a valuation premium.

## The Dangerous Moment

The narrative is most dangerous when it feels most obviously true. In late 1999, "the internet will change everything" was obviously true — the internet DID change everything. But the stocks still fell 80%. The narrative was correct, but the price had already exceeded what even the correct narrative justified.

Distinguish between the narrative being true and the narrative being priced in. They are different things, and confusing them is where the losses are.
