# Reflexivity — Markets Do Not Just Reflect Reality, They Create It

## Soros's Big Idea

George Soros's theory of reflexivity, developed over decades of trading and articulated in "The Alchemy of Finance" (1987), challenges the foundational assumption of economics: that markets are a mirror reflecting underlying fundamentals. Soros argued that markets are not a mirror but a feedback loop — market prices influence the fundamentals they are supposed to reflect, and those changed fundamentals in turn influence prices.

This is not a metaphor. It is a precise description of how asset prices, credit conditions, and corporate behavior interact.

## The Positive Feedback Loop

When a company's stock price rises:

1. **The company can raise capital more cheaply.** Secondary offerings at high prices dilute existing shareholders less. Convertible bond terms are more favorable. The cost of equity capital drops.
2. **The company can attract better talent.** Stock-based compensation is worth more. Top engineers and executives join because the equity package is attractive.
3. **The company can acquire competitors.** Using its inflated stock as currency, it can buy smaller companies at a premium that is still cheap relative to its own valuation.
4. **Customers and partners gain confidence.** A company with a rising stock price is perceived as stable, innovative, and growing. Contracts and partnerships flow more easily.
5. **Analysts raise targets.** Higher stock price leads to higher price targets, which leads to more buying, which leads to a higher stock price.

The rising stock price genuinely IMPROVES the company's competitive position and fundamental outlook. This is not just sentiment — it is a real change in the company's capabilities and prospects. The stock price is not reflecting reality; it is shaping it.

## The Negative Feedback Loop

The reverse is equally powerful and more vicious:

1. **The company's cost of capital rises.** Borrowing becomes more expensive. Equity issuance at low prices is extremely dilutive.
2. **Talent leaves.** Stock-based compensation is underwater. Key employees exercise early exit clauses or simply leave for companies with better equity packages.
3. **Partners and customers get nervous.** A falling stock price raises questions about viability. Enterprise customers hesitate to sign long-term contracts. Partners seek alternatives.
4. **Credit facilities tighten.** Lenders invoke covenants tied to market cap or stock price. Revolving credit lines are reduced precisely when cash is needed most.
5. **Short sellers smell blood.** A declining stock attracts short interest, which adds selling pressure, which drives the price lower, which attracts more short sellers.

The falling stock price genuinely WORSENS the company's competitive position and fundamental outlook. The doom loop is self-reinforcing.

## Reflexivity in Broader Markets

The concept extends beyond individual stocks:

**Housing (2003-2008):** Rising house prices allowed homeowners to borrow more (home equity loans), which they spent on consumption, which boosted GDP, which supported employment, which supported more house purchases, which pushed prices higher. The feedback loop ran until the debt burden became unsustainable. Then it reversed catastrophically.

**Sovereign debt crises:** Rising government bond yields increase interest costs, which worsen the fiscal deficit, which reduces creditworthiness, which pushes yields higher. Greece experienced this doom loop in 2010-2012, where the market's fear of default actually made default more likely.

**Currency crises:** A falling currency increases the cost of foreign-denominated debt, which weakens corporate balance sheets, which triggers capital flight, which weakens the currency further. This pattern has repeated in emerging markets for decades.

## Trading Implications

Reflexivity means:
- **Trends persist longer than fundamentals justify** because the rising price improves the fundamentals, which justifies the higher price. This is why momentum works.
- **Reversals are violent** because the declining price worsens the fundamentals, which justifies the lower price. This is why mean reversion can be a death trap in a reflexive decline.
- **Fair value is not static.** The "correct" price depends on what the price IS, because the price changes the underlying reality.

For the agent: identify when reflexive loops are operating. In positive loops, ride momentum but monitor for the tipping point where debt or valuation becomes unsustainable. In negative loops, do not try to catch the falling knife — the fundamentals are getting worse BECAUSE the price is falling, and they will not stabilize until the feedback loop exhausts itself.
