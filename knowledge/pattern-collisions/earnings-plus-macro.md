# Earnings Plus Macro: Trading the Collision Between Company and Economy

## Core Principle

Every stock trades at the intersection of two forces: the company's specific performance (earnings) and the broad economic environment (macro). The most common mistake is analyzing one while ignoring the other. Great earnings in a tightening cycle have limited upside because the macro headwind caps the move. Bad earnings in an easing cycle have limited downside because the macro tailwind provides a floor. Understanding this collision determines position sizing, direction, and duration.

## The Four Quadrants

### Quadrant 1: Strong Earnings + Bullish Macro (Both Aligned Bullish)
This is the highest-conviction long setup. The company is executing AND the environment is supportive. Rate cuts, expanding liquidity, and strong earnings create the conditions for multi-month trends. Position sizing can be aggressive. These are the setups that produce 50-100% moves in individual stocks over 6-12 months.

**Action:** Size up. Hold longer. Add on pullbacks. The trend has both engines firing.

### Quadrant 2: Strong Earnings + Bearish Macro (Mixed — Macro Headwind)
The company is doing everything right, but the economic environment is working against it. Higher rates, tightening liquidity, or recessionary conditions limit the upside from great results. The stock might pop on earnings, but the rally fades within days because the macro gravity pulls it back down.

**Action:** Trade the pop, do not hold for a trend. Take profits quickly. The company is good but the environment will not let it run. If you must hold, reduce size significantly and set tight stops.

### Quadrant 3: Weak Earnings + Bullish Macro (Mixed — Macro Tailwind)
The company missed, but the environment is forgiving. Rate cuts, QE, or fiscal stimulus provide a floor under even mediocre companies. The stock drops on the miss but finds buyers quickly because capital is abundant and looking for somewhere to go. Bad companies in bull markets often recover within weeks.

**Action:** Do not short. The macro tailwind will bail out the company. If you are long, hold through the weakness — the environment will carry it. This is the "rising tide lifts all boats" scenario, even the leaky ones.

### Quadrant 4: Weak Earnings + Bearish Macro (Both Aligned Bearish)
This is the highest-conviction short setup. The company is failing AND the environment is hostile. This combination produces the waterfall declines — the 30-50% drops that happen over weeks, not months. There is no floor because neither the company nor the environment provides support.

**Action:** Size up on the short side. Let it ride. This is where fortunes are made on the short side. The trend has both gravity wells pulling downward.

## Magnitude and Duration Rules

**Both aligned (Q1 or Q4):** Larger magnitude moves, longer duration trends. The forces compound each other. Expect trends to last 2-6 months before a significant counter-move.

**Mixed signals (Q2 or Q3):** Smaller magnitude moves, shorter duration. The forces partially cancel each other out. Expect moves to last days to weeks before reverting. Range-bound trading is common in these quadrants.

## The Collision Timing

Earnings reports happen quarterly. Macro regime shifts happen less frequently. The critical moments are:

1. **Earnings during a macro regime change:** A company reports great Q4 numbers, but between reporting date and the next quarter, the Fed pivots hawkish. The stock trades as Q1 (earnings strong + macro strong) but shifts to Q2 (earnings strong + macro turning). This transition is where many traders get trapped — they hold based on the earnings quality without recognizing the macro regime has changed.

2. **Macro shift mid-earnings season:** When the Fed makes a surprise move during earnings season, it changes the interpretation of every subsequent report. A company that would have rallied on an earnings beat in an easing cycle might sell off on the same beat in a suddenly tightening cycle.

## Practical Application

Before every earnings trade, ask two questions:
1. Is the company likely to beat or miss? (Earnings analysis)
2. Is the current macro environment helping or hurting this sector? (Macro analysis)

Then place yourself in the correct quadrant and let that determine your sizing, direction, and holding period. Never let a great earnings thesis override a hostile macro environment, and never let a scary macro environment prevent you from holding a company with both engines firing.
