# Congressional Trading: The Legal Inside Track

## The Edge

Members of the United States Congress consistently outperform the stock market because they trade on non-public legislative and regulatory knowledge. They sit on committees that oversee industries, receive classified briefings, draft regulations, and vote on bills that move entire sectors — then trade stocks in those same sectors.

In 2024, Democratic lawmakers averaged roughly 31% returns while Republicans averaged 26%, compared to the S&P 500 return of 24.9%. But that headline number masks the real story: congressional *leaders* — committee chairs, whips, speakers — outperform rank-and-file members by up to 47% per year according to a National Bureau of Economic Research working paper. Their stock sales are particularly predictive, forecasting negative corporate news events including hearings and regulatory actions in the following year.

## The STOCK Act (2012)

The Stop Trading on Congressional Knowledge Act was supposed to fix this. It requires members and senior staff to disclose trades within 45 days. In practice, it changed almost nothing:

- Violations are routine and penalties are negligible ($200 fines, often waived)
- 45-day disclosure delay means the trade is stale by the time the public sees it
- Enforcement is effectively non-existent — the SEC has never brought a case under the STOCK Act
- A 2013 amendment quietly gutted the online disclosure requirement for senior staff

## Nancy Pelosi as Case Study

Pelosi became the poster child for congressional trading. Her husband Paul Pelosi made large, well-timed call option purchases in companies like Alphabet, Nvidia, and Disney — often shortly before favorable legislation or regulatory decisions. Her portfolio consistently outperformed most hedge funds. Whether this is skill, coincidence, or information advantage is debated, but the timing patterns are statistically difficult to explain.

## How to Track and Trade

Congressional trades are filed with the Clerk of the House and Secretary of the Senate. Several services aggregate this data:

- **Unusual Whales** (unusualwhales.com/congress) — free tracking with delay
- **Quiver Quantitative** (quiverquant.com/congresstrading) — historical data and analytics
- **Capitol Trades** — real-time alerts on filings
- **Kapitol.ai** — AI-powered analysis of congressional trading patterns

The strategy: monitor trades by committee leaders in sectors their committees oversee. A Senate Banking Committee member buying bank stocks before a regulatory vote is the signal. The 45-day lag means you are trading on delayed information, but academic research shows that even copying trades at the disclosure date still generates 3-6% annualised alpha.

## What the Agent Should Know

This is not a conspiracy theory — it is empirically documented. The alpha exists because the penalty structure makes it rational to trade on legislative knowledge. The STOCK Act created disclosure requirements without meaningful enforcement. Track the filings, weight toward leadership trades, and pay special attention to trades that cluster around upcoming committee hearings.
