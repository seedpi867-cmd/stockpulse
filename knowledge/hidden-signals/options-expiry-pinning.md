# Options Expiry Pinning: The Gravitational Pull of Open Interest

## The Phenomenon

On options expiration Fridays — monthly (third Friday) and especially quarterly (March, June, September, December) — stocks have a measurable tendency to close near the strike price with the highest open interest. This is called "pinning" and it is not random. It is the mechanical result of delta hedging by market makers.

## Why It Happens

When you buy a call option, the market maker who sold it hedges by buying shares of the underlying stock. As expiration approaches, the delta of at-the-money options swings wildly. If the stock is just above a strike, the market maker is long shares (hedging calls). If it dips below, they sell. If it pops above, they buy again.

This delta hedging creates a gravitational effect: stock moves above the strike and market makers sell, pushing it back down. Stock moves below the strike and market makers buy, pushing it back up. The more open interest at a strike, the stronger this gravitational pull. On high-volume expiration days, billions of dollars in hedging flows converge on a small number of strikes.

## Max Pain Theory

Max pain is the price at which the total value of all outstanding options contracts is minimised — the price at which option buyers collectively lose the most money. The theory says stocks gravitate toward max pain at expiration because market makers (who are net short options) profit most at that price.

Max pain is calculated by summing the intrinsic value of all calls and puts at each strike and finding the strike where the total is lowest. Free calculators exist on sites like Maximum-Pain.com and options analytics platforms.

## The Hierarchy of Expiry Events

Not all expirations are equal. Daily and weekly expirations have minimal effect due to limited open interest. Monthly expirations (OpEx) create noticeable pinning, especially in high-volume names. Quarterly expirations (quad witching) are the big ones — stock options, index options, stock futures, and index futures all expire simultaneously. Trillions in notional value. This is when pinning is strongest and intraday volatility spikes wildly in the final hour. LEAPS expiration in January, when long-dated options roll off, can also create unusual moves.

## How to Trade Around It

First, do not fight the pin. If a stock is near max pain on expiry Friday morning, expect it to stay there. Directional bets on expiry day often get chopped up.

Second, sell premium into expiry. Pinning benefits option sellers. Short straddles or iron condors centred on the max pain strike exploit the dampened movement.

Third, watch for the unpin. After 4 PM when options expire, the hedging pressure vanishes. Monday can see the real move that was suppressed on Friday. Some of the sharpest Monday gaps follow Friday pins.

Fourth, track open interest changes. Large open interest building at a specific strike through the week tells you where the magnet is. If a strike goes from 5,000 to 50,000 contracts of open interest, that strike becomes a gravity well.

## What the Agent Should Know

Calendar-aware trading is essential. Mark monthly and quarterly expiration dates. Calculate max pain for positions. Avoid initiating short-term directional trades on expiry Friday. Watch for the post-expiry release Monday through Wednesday as the real directional move emerges.
