# Corporate Buybacks: The Biggest Buyer You Never See

## The Scale

S&P 500 companies spent a record $942.5 billion on share buybacks in 2024, up 18.5% from $795 billion in 2023. To put this in perspective, that is more than the entire GDP of most countries. Corporations are consistently the single largest net buyer of US equities — not retail investors, not pension funds, not hedge funds. Companies buying their own stock.

Information technology leads (27% of all buybacks), followed by financials (18.5%) and communication services (15.1%). Apple alone has bought back over $700 billion of its own stock since 2012.

## How Buybacks Move Prices

Buybacks create a mechanical bid under the stock. When a company buys back shares:

1. **Float reduction** — fewer shares outstanding means each remaining share represents a larger slice of the company. A company earning $1 billion with 1 billion shares has $1 EPS. Buy back 100 million shares and EPS jumps to $1.11 — an 11% increase with zero actual growth.

2. **EPS inflation** — because most executive compensation is tied to EPS targets, buybacks are the easiest way for management to hit bonuses without growing the business. This is the dirty secret Wall Street does not discuss: a significant portion of EPS growth over the last decade is financial engineering, not real growth.

3. **Momentum trigger** — sustained buying pressure pushes the stock up, which triggers momentum algorithms, which brings in more buyers. Buybacks create their own positive feedback loop.

4. **Volatility suppression** — a constant bid dampens selloffs. Companies often accelerate buybacks during dips, creating a floor.

## The Announcement vs Execution Gap

This is where the real edge lives. Companies *announce* buyback programmes with great fanfare — "$10 billion buyback authorised!" — and get the stock pop. But there is no requirement to actually execute. Many companies announce buybacks and never complete them, or only buy back a fraction.

To tell real from fake:
- Check 10-Q filings for actual shares repurchased vs authorised
- Compare cash flow from operations to buyback spending — if they are borrowing to buy back stock, it is more aggressive
- Watch for Rule 10b-18 safe harbour filings — these show daily buyback activity
- Companies with "accelerated share repurchase" (ASR) agreements with banks are serious — they are locking in
- If a company announces a buyback while insiders are selling, the buyback is providing exit liquidity for management

## Blackout Windows

Companies cannot buy back stock during the weeks before earnings (the "blackout window"). This means buyback demand disappears for roughly 4-6 weeks per quarter. The absence of the biggest buyer creates a seasonal vulnerability in stock prices. Some of the worst selloffs happen during blackout periods when the corporate bid vanishes.

## What the Agent Should Know

Track buyback announcements against actual execution. Weight companies that are spending >3% of market cap on buybacks annually — that level of float reduction is material. Be aware of blackout windows as periods of elevated downside risk. And always ask: is EPS growing because the business is growing, or because the denominator is shrinking?
