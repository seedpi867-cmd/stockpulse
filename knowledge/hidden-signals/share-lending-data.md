# Share Lending Data: The Cost to Borrow as a Predictive Signal

## What Share Lending Reveals

When a trader wants to short a stock, they must borrow shares from a broker or institutional lender. The cost to borrow those shares is set by supply and demand. When many traders want to short the same stock and the available float is limited, the borrow cost spikes. This cost-to-borrow data is one of the most underused predictive signals in the market.

## How Borrow Cost Works

Most large-cap stocks are "easy to borrow" at negligible cost — under 1% annualised. The shares are plentiful because large institutions (pension funds, index funds, insurance companies) lend their holdings to earn extra yield.

When borrow cost rises above 5%, it means short demand is elevated. Above 20%, it is extremely elevated. Above 50-100%, the stock is in "hard to borrow" territory and a squeeze setup is forming. Some meme stocks have seen borrow costs exceed 500% annualised.

The cost is quoted as an annualised rate. A 100% borrow cost means the short seller pays roughly 0.27% per day just to maintain the position. At these levels, shorts are under extreme pressure to cover because the carrying cost erodes any profit from the stock declining.

## The Squeeze Setup

The classic short squeeze setup requires three ingredients. High short interest (above 20% of float) means many shares are sold short relative to available supply. High borrow cost (above 20% annualised) means demand to short exceeds available supply. A catalyst — positive earnings surprise, analyst upgrade, social media attention, or a fundamental shift — provides the ignition.

When a catalyst hits and the stock starts rising, shorts face losses on their position plus escalating borrow costs. They are forced to buy to cover, which pushes the price higher, which forces more shorts to cover. The feedback loop is the squeeze.

GameStop in January 2021 was the extreme example: over 100% of float was short (due to rehypothecation), borrow costs spiked to triple digits, and a retail-driven catalyst triggered the most famous squeeze in market history.

## Where to Find the Data

Cost-to-borrow data is not free. Primary sources include ORTEX, S3 Partners, Interactive Brokers (which shows borrow availability and cost for its customers), and Fintel. Some data is delayed by a day or more depending on the provider.

Key metrics to track: short interest as a percentage of float, days to cover (short interest divided by average daily volume), borrow cost trend (rising borrow cost is more significant than static high cost), and utilisation rate (percentage of available shares that are currently lent out — 100% utilisation means no more shares available to lend).

## The Contrarian Signal

Extremely high short interest is also a contrarian bullish signal even without a squeeze. Academic research shows that the most heavily shorted stocks underperform (shorts are often right), but when they do rally, they rally harder than any other category because of forced covering. The risk-reward is asymmetric.

## What the Agent Should Know

Monitor borrow cost and short interest for all positions and watchlist stocks. Rising borrow cost is an early warning of crowded shorts. The combination of high short interest, high borrow cost, and high utilisation creates the conditions for explosive upside moves. The agent should flag stocks entering "hard to borrow" territory and assess whether a catalyst could trigger forced covering.
