# Seasonal Patterns: Calendar Anomalies That Actually Work

## The Big Five

Stock market seasonality is dismissed by efficient market theorists but embraced by practitioners because most of these patterns have persisted for decades, surviving publication and widespread knowledge. They should not work in theory. They do in practice.

### 1. Sell in May and Go Away (The Halloween Indicator)

From November through April, the S&P 500 has historically returned roughly 7% on average. From May through October, roughly 2%. This pattern has held across over 100 years of data and across international markets. The Bouman and Jacobsen (2002) study in the American Economic Review confirmed it across 37 countries.

Why it persists: reduced institutional trading volume in summer, vacation schedules thinning liquidity, and corporate earnings cycles concentrating positive catalysts in Q4 and Q1.

### 2. The January Effect

Small-cap stocks outperform large caps in January, typically by 2-5 percentage points. The mechanism is well understood: tax-loss harvesting. In December, investors sell losing positions to realise capital losses for tax purposes. This depresses small-cap prices artificially. In January, buying returns and these beaten-down stocks bounce.

The effect has weakened since publication but has not disappeared. It is most pronounced in micro-caps and in years following market downturns.

### 3. The Santa Claus Rally

The last five trading days of December and first two of January historically produce positive returns about 75% of the time, averaging roughly 1.3%. Yale Hirsch of the Stock Trader's Almanac coined the term and tracked it from 1969 onward.

More important is the negative signal: when the Santa Claus rally fails, it often precedes a weak first quarter. As Hirsch wrote, "If Santa Claus should fail to call, bears may come to Broad and Wall."

### 4. September: The Worst Month

September is the only month with a negative average return going back to 1928. Average loss is roughly 1%. The reasons are debated — end of fiscal year for mutual funds, return from summer vacations triggering portfolio reviews, and psychological reset after Labor Day.

Major crashes and crises have disproportionately struck in September-October: 1929, 1987, 2001, 2008.

### 5. The Pre-Holiday Effect

Markets tend to rise on the trading day before major US holidays (Thanksgiving, Christmas, Independence Day, Memorial Day). Average pre-holiday returns are significantly higher than normal trading days. Lower volume and a positive sentiment bias drive this — bearish traders close positions before the break.

## Tax-Loss Harvesting Mechanics

The December-January dynamic deserves deeper explanation. In December, investors dump losers. This creates artificial selling pressure concentrated in the last two weeks. The stocks most affected are small caps, recent IPOs that have fallen, and any stock down significantly year-to-date.

Savvy traders buy these December orphans in the last week of December and hold through January. The strategy has a name: the "January Rebound." It works best in years where the market has been volatile and many stocks have losses to harvest.

## What the Agent Should Know

Calendar awareness is free alpha. Weight seasonal tendencies into position sizing and timing. Do not fight September weakness or chase May rallies. The patterns are not guaranteed in any single year but over a portfolio of years they are statistically significant.
